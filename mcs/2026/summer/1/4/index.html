Mini-Lesson · One Big Idea

What is a loop?

Without a loop — painful!
move 10 steps
move 10 steps
move 10 steps
move 10 steps
...do this 10 times?! 😩

10 lines for 1 simple idea. Easy to forget one.

With a loop — elegant!
repeat 10
  move 10 steps
end ✅ same result!

3 lines. Change the 10 to 100 — done!

Two loop types in Scratch
repeat 10 Does it exactly N times, then stops.
forever Never stops! Runs until you stop it.

In Scratch, loops are in the Control (orange) block section.

When might you want forever vs repeat 10? Think of a real-life example!

A loop is an instruction that says: "Do this group of steps multiple times." It's one of the most powerful tools in all of programming.

Quick Check · Slide 5 of 16

Which loop would you use?

Scenario A: A sprite waves hello exactly 3 times when clicked.

Correct! repeat 3 stops exactly when the job is done. Forever would wave forever — and never stop!
Almost! If you use forever, the sprite never stops waving. You'd need to hit the stop button. repeat 3 is the right pick.

Scenario B: A bouncing ball keeps moving until the player quits the game.

Yes! forever means the ball moves as long as the game is running — no fixed count needed. That's perfect for games!
Good try! But what if the player plays for more than 100 bounces? forever means the ball never runs out of bounces on its own.
The key question to ask yourself:

Does this action have a fixed count (use repeat N), or does it need to run until the user stops the program (use forever)?

AI Moment · "AI learns by repeating"

How does AI practice? It loops!

How a musician practices
  1. Play the hard part of the song
  2. Notice the mistake
  3. Adjust fingers / timing
  4. Repeat — loop back to step 1!
How an AI "practices"
repeat 1000 times:
  look at an example
  make a guess
  check if guess is right
  adjust (learn from mistake)
→ gets better each loop!
The loop connection

When an AI learns to recognize a cat photo, it doesn't study one picture once. It loops through thousands of cat photos — over and over — each time getting a little bit better at spotting what makes a cat look like a cat.

More loops = more practice = smarter AI. Just like practicing piano!

? If the AI only looked at 3 cat photos, would it be good at recognizing cats? What would happen if it looped through 10,000 photos instead?

Today's code concept = today's AI idea: Your repeat block IS the "training loop" of an AI — the core engine that makes patterns get stronger the more times it runs.

Guided Build · Step 1 of 3 · 20 min total

Draw a square with repeat 4

Build this together in Scratch
when 🏁 clicked
pen down
repeat 4
  move 100 steps
  turn 90 degrees
end
Steps to set up:
1
Open Scratch → choose or keep the cat sprite
2
Click Add Extension → add Pen
3
Drag when flag clicked → pen down → repeat 4
4
Inside repeat: move 100 steps → turn 90 degrees
5
Click the green flag — a square appears!
Why does this work?

A square has 4 equal sides and 4 right-angle turns (90° each). Instead of writing "move 100, turn 90" four times, the loop does it all in three lines.

4 sides × 90° = 360° total turn = a complete square!

Extension experiments:
  • Change 4 to 3 + 120° → triangle!
  • Change to 6 + 60° → hexagon!
  • Change to 36 + 10° → almost a circle!
Tier A

Just use repeat 4 and make the square — that's the whole goal!

Tier C

Try all three shapes. What's the pattern between sides and degrees?

Guided Build · Step 2 of 3

Make a sprite dance forever

Build this in Scratch
when 🏁 clicked
forever
  next costume
  wait 0.3 seconds
end (loops until stop)

The Scratch cat has multiple costumes. Switching them fast creates animation!

Why does the dance look smooth?

Because the forever loop keeps cycling through costumes without ever stopping. Movies work the same way — 24 images per second, looping forward.

Try changing 0.3 to 0.1 — does the dance speed up?

Challenge it!
  • Tier B Add a move 10 steps + if on edge bounce so the sprite dances AND walks.
  • Tier C Add a sound block inside the loop so the sprite plays a beat while dancing.

Challenge Card · Nested Loops

Loops inside loops = patterns inside patterns

Flower of squares (nested)
when 🏁 clicked
repeat 6 ← outer: 6 petals
  repeat 4 ← inner: draw square
    move 80 steps
    turn 90 degrees
  turn 60 degrees
end

These mandala snowflakes are exactly what nested loops create — a shape repeated and rotated around a center.

? How many total move steps does this program run? (Outer × inner = ?)
Count it together:
→
Outer loop runs 6 times
→
Each time outer runs, inner runs 4 times
✓
6 × 4 = 24 total move steps = 6 squares drawn!
