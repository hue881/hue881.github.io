🧮 Part 1 of 5 · 8 min
Game Theory Warm-Up: Why Can't RPS Be "Solved"?
🎲 The Nash Equilibrium Puzzle

In Rock-Paper-Scissors, the mathematically optimal strategy is to play rock, paper, and scissors each with exactly 1/3 probability, completely at random. This is called the Nash Equilibrium — no other fixed strategy beats it in the long run.

Question 1: If a player always throws rock, what is the optimal counter-strategy, and what is your expected win rate against them?

Question 2: If both players play perfect 1/3-1/3-1/3 randomness, what is each player's expected win rate? Why can no strategy do better against this opponent?

🧠 The catch: Humans are bad at being truly random — we unconsciously repeat patterns. A predictor that studies a human's history can win more than 33% of rounds. That is exactly what you will build in Part 2.

Build a Markov-Chain Move Predictor
📋 The Idea

Instead of counting which move a player uses most overall (Tier C), track which move they play after each previous move. Store this as a dictionary of dictionaries: transitions[previous_move][next_move] = count. Then predict whatever move most often follows the opponent's last move, and counter it.
