# [AI & Coding Summer Program](https://hue881.github.io/mcs/2026/summer) — Syllabus
**Modern Chinese School | Summer 2026**
**Python, Scratch & Artificial Intelligence | Ages 7–13 (Grades 2–9)**



## Course Description

This summer program introduces students ages 7–13 to computer programming and
artificial intelligence using Scratch (block-based coding) and Python (text-based
coding). Because no enrolled student has prior Python experience and most are new to AI,
the course begins at absolute zero and builds, step by step, toward a real project each
student can show their family. Students start in Scratch to learn coding logic without
syntax stress, bridge into Python fundamentals, and end by building simple AI programs
such as rule-based chatbots and keyword classifiers. AI is treated not as magic but as
algorithms — clear sets of rules a computer follows — and is woven into every week.
All Python work uses the standard library only, with no outside packages required.

The program runs Monday through Thursday, June 29 through August 14, 2026, in three
mixed-age sections of about 110 minutes each. The format is mixed: structured
instruction paired with open, hands-on creative time.



## Student Learning Objectives

By the end of the program, students will be able to:

1. Explain, in their own words, what an algorithm is and what AI is.
2. Build a working Scratch project using events, loops, conditionals, and variables.
3. Write basic Python programs using print(), input(), variables, and math operators.
4. Use if/elif/else statements and loops to control how a program behaves.
5. Create and use functions and lists to organize a program.
6. Build a simple AI program (rule-based chatbot or keyword classifier) and explain
   the algorithm behind it.
7. Describe the core AI ideas of training data, models, predictions, and accuracy.
8. Discuss, at an age-appropriate level, when AI can be wrong and why that matters.
9. Complete and present a capstone project to a partner or family member.



## Course Etiquette

- Be kind and patient — everyone in this class is a beginner.
- Mistakes are part of coding; bugs are normal and expected.
- No one is ever forced to present in front of the group; sharing is always optional.
- Respect each other's screens, projects, and ideas.
- Take movement breaks when offered, and help a neighbor when you can.
- Phones and outside devices are put away during instruction time.



## Class Participation / Effort

Participation is measured by effort and engagement, not by being the fastest or the
most advanced. Students are expected to:

- Try the activity, even when it feels hard, before asking for the answer.
- Complete the short (~30 min) homework after each session.
- Use the optional share-out and pair time to support classmates.
- Bring questions and curiosity to the daily tech check-in.

Effort is celebrated. Students who finish early are encouraged to attempt the
"challenge path" or to mentor a younger classmate rather than wait.



## Course Format

Each ~110-minute session follows a flexible, mixed rhythm:

- Tech check-in (~10 min): a cool piece of AI/coding news or a quick warm-up.
- Direct instruction (~20 min): one new concept, demonstrated live.
- Guided coding (~30 min): students follow along and build together.
- Open project time (~20 min): creative exploration and personal projects.
- Optional share-out (~15 min): never required.
- Exit ticket (~5 min): "one thing I built today."

Every lesson runs on three parallel tracks so the wide age range stays engaged:
- Track A — Scratch (Grades 2–5)
- Track B — Python Beginner (Grades 5–6)
- Track C — Python + AI (Grades 7–9)

(Note: the 10:30 AM section ends at lunch (~11:45), so it runs a shorter version —
core instruction and guided practice, with open project time moved to homework.)



## Required Readings

There is no textbook. All materials are provided in class. Students should have access to:

- The course's printed handouts and reference sheets (provided each week).
- Scratch (https://scratch.mit.edu) — free, browser-based.
- Python Playground (https://playcode.io/python-playground) — free, browser-based.
- Google Teachable Machine (teachablemachine.withgoogle.com) — used for live AI demos.

Optional at-home enrichment may be suggested week to week, but nothing outside class
is mandatory.



## Evaluation

Students are evaluated on growth and completion, not on competing with one another:

| Component | Weight | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Participation & effort | 25% | Engagement, trying hard, helping peers |
| Homework completion | 25% | ~30 min after each session |
| Midterm check-in | 15% | Low-stakes; July 13–16 window |
| Final check-in | 15% | Low-stakes; Aug 3–6 window |
| Capstone project | 20% | Graded on the project, not the presentation |

Assessments are designed to be low-pressure and confidence-building. Students with
test or presentation anxiety may demonstrate learning through their project alone.



## AI Policy

- AI is a subject we study and build in this class, treated as algorithms, not magic.
- Students will use AI demos (e.g., Teachable Machine) and build simple AI programs
  themselves in Python and Scratch.
- Students should not use outside AI chatbots to complete their homework for them;
  the goal is to learn how AI works by building it, not to have it do the work.
- All AI activities are age-appropriate, and a class discussion on AI fairness and
  limitations ("Is AI ever wrong?") is included. Parents have indicated full comfort
  with AI being taught.
- Weekly parent email updates will be sent; families may request immediate contact
  if a student is struggling.
