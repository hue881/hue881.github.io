## Course Description

This summer program introduces students ages 7–13 to computer programming and artificial intelligence using Scratch (block-based coding) and Python (text-based coding). Designed directly from a 28-survey intake analysis, the cohort spans Grade 1 to Grade 9 and is near-greenfield in Python (82% never tried) and AI (68% never tried). The course begins at absolute zero and builds step by step toward a real project each student can show their family.

Students start in Scratch to learn coding logic without syntax stress, bridge into Python fundamentals, and end by building simple AI programs such as rule-based chatbots and keyword classifiers. AI is treated not as magic but as algorithms — clear sets of rules a computer follows — and is woven into every single session. All Python work uses the standard library only, with no outside packages required.

The program follows a **spiral, project-first curriculum** taught at **three differentiated tiers** with the **same daily rhythm every day**. Parents prize problem-solving and creative projects over any one language, and this course answers that directly.



## Student Learning Objectives

By the end of the program, students will be able to:

1. Explain, in their own words, what an algorithm is and what AI is.
2. Build a working Scratch project using events, loops, conditionals, and variables.
3. Write basic Python programs using `print()`, `input()`, variables, and math operators.
4. Use `if/elif/else` statements and loops to control how a program behaves.
5. Create and use functions and lists to organize a program.
6. Build a simple AI program (rule-based chatbot or keyword classifier) and explain the algorithm behind it.
7. Describe the core AI ideas of training data, models, predictions, and accuracy.
8. Discuss, at an age-appropriate level, when AI can be wrong and why that matters.
9. Complete and present a capstone project to a partner or family member.



## The Three Tiers (same project, three entry points)

Every section contains a mix of all three tiers. Students get the **same daily concept and the same project**, with three on-ramps. **Challenge cards** (an optional harder version of each task) allow fast finishers to extend instead of waiting — resolving the survey's central tension: 43% fear *falling behind* while 18% fear *boredom*.

| Tier | Who | How they work |
|---|---|---|
| **Tier A — Foundations** | Grades 1–3 / no experience | More scaffolding, stay in Scratch longer, paired with a buddy, Python introduced gently through `turtle`. Start with internet safety + responsible-AI basics. Encouragement-first; never criticized publicly. |
| **Tier B — Core** | Grades 3–5, some Scratch | The main track exactly as sequenced. This is the majority of the room (~54% are Grades 3–4). |
| **Tier C — Accelerated** | Grades 6–9 / Python-curious | Move to Python faster, always take the challenge card, get stretch goals and a light intro to project tooling (file organization, a gentle GitHub peek for advanced students). |



## Course Etiquette

- Be kind and patient — everyone in this class is a beginner.
- Mistakes are part of coding; bugs are normal and expected.
- No one is ever forced to present in front of the group; sharing is always optional.
- Respect each other's screens, projects, and ideas.
- Take movement breaks when offered, and help a neighbor when you can.
- Phones and outside devices are put away during instruction time.



## Class Participation / Effort

Participation is measured by effort and engagement, not by being the fastest or the most advanced. Students are expected to:

- Try the activity, even when it feels hard, before asking for the answer.
- Complete the short (~30 min) homework after each session.
- Use the optional share-out and pair time to support classmates.
- Bring questions and curiosity to every session.

Effort is celebrated. Students who finish early are encouraged to attempt the "challenge path" or to mentor a younger classmate rather than wait.



## Daily Rhythm (every session, all three sections)

Structure is the backbone (68% of students want structure), with exploration (50%) and friendly challenge (46%) layered in, plus movement breaks built in for students who can't sit for long stretches.

| Time | Block | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 10 min | **Warm-up** — unplugged logic puzzle or "code of the day," no screen | Brains on; leans on the math/logic strength |
| 15–20 min | **Mini-lesson** — one concept, demoed live, kept short | Low cognitive load, one idea at a time |
| 20 min | **Guided build** — everyone builds it together, step by step | Structure; guaranteed early success |
| 5 min | **Movement / brain break** | Essential focus support |
| 30–40 min | **Open build + challenge cards** | Apply concept to own project; explore & stretch |
| 10 min | **Share-out** — 1–2 volunteers or "show your neighbor" | Low-stakes, opt-in, warm |

> **🤖 AI Moment (built into every day):** Each session has a 5–10 minute AI tie-in folded into the mini-lesson or warm-up — never a separate lecture. It connects that day's coding concept to how AI actually works (data, patterns, rules, prediction, bias, ethics). These are marked **🤖** throughout the schedule.

> **Section 1 compression (75 min):** Warm-up (5) → Mini-lesson + 🤖 AI Moment (15) → Guided build (20) → Brain break (5) → Open build (25) → Quick share (5). Homework is the same as other sections.



## Course Phases (spiral: concrete → text → abstract)

The sequence moves from visual/concrete to text to abstract, matching comfort data and learning science (low → high cognitive load).

- **Phase 0 — Welcome & Foundations** (Sessions 1–2): course intro, icebreakers, accounts, internet safety, "what is AI really."
- **Phase 1 — Scratch as the on-ramp** (Sessions 3–7): sequencing, loops, conditionals, variables, events. Every student ships a tiny interactive project by end of Week 2.
- **Phase 2 — Bridge Scratch → Python** (Sessions 8–14, around the midterm): rebuild Scratch logic in Python text; `turtle` graphics and tiny text games.
- **Phase 3 — AI made tangible** (Sessions 15–20): Teachable Machine, a rule-based Python chatbot, AI ethics through stories, and how real AI differs.
- **Phase 4 — Capstone** (Sessions 21–26): students build a chosen project (game / AI mini-app / story) and present at a low-stakes showcase.

## Evaluation

Students are evaluated on growth and completion, not on competing with one another. Graded on **growth, effort, and the ability to explain** — not on perfection.

| Component | Weight | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Daily participation & homework | 30% | Consistent effort; the 30-min nightly tasks |
| Midterm (Jul 15) | 20% | Core concepts + first AI understanding |
| Final (Aug 4) | 20% | Applied concepts + capstone progress demo |
| Capstone project | 20% | A real project the student is proud of |
| Reflection & growth | 10% | Self-awareness; "can explain a loop/variable/training data" |

Assessments are designed to be low-pressure and confidence-building. Students with test or presentation anxiety may demonstrate learning through their project alone. Praise effort and process publicly; give corrections privately.

**Program-wide success metrics:**
- Every student ships ≥1 working project in Week 2 and a chosen capstone by the end.
- Each student can explain, in their own words, what a loop / conditional / variable is **and** "what AI training data is."
- Tier C students complete ≥1 Python project; Tier A students complete ≥1 Scratch project and one tiny Python (`turtle`) program.



## AI Policy

- AI is a subject we study and build in this class, treated as algorithms, not magic.
- Students will use AI demos (e.g., Teachable Machine) and build simple AI programs themselves in Python and Scratch.
- Students should not use outside AI chatbots to complete their homework for them; the goal is to learn how AI works by building it, not to have it do the work.
- All AI activities are age-appropriate, and a class discussion on AI fairness and limitations ("Is AI ever wrong?") is included.
- Weekly parent email updates will be sent; families may request immediate contact if a student is struggling.



## AI Integration Map (one AI idea per session)

Every session has an AI tie-in, building from "AI is just instructions" to "AI learns from data, and that data can be biased."

| Sessions | AI Thread |
|---|---|
| 1–2 | AI follows instructions; responsible & safe AI use |
| 3–7 | Code concepts mirror AI ideas: sequence→training steps, loops→practice, conditionals→decision trees/classifiers, variables→memory & confidence |
| 8–14 | Text & data: chatbots as input→output, datasets as lists, search strategies, rules vs. learning |
| 15 | **Teachable Machine** — training data → model → prediction, hands-on |
| 16–17 | Rule-based chatbot vs. LLMs; **bias, fairness, privacy** through a hands-on misclassification story |

### Week 1 — Welcome & First Wins (Jun 29 – Jul 2)

**Session 1 · Mon Jun 29 — Welcome, Icebreakers & "What Is a Computer Thinking?"**
- *Focus:* Course introduction, expectations, icebreakers, team norms. Mostly social, low pressure.
- *Activities:* "Two truths and a robot lie" icebreaker; tour of tools; set up Scratch accounts; class agreements (encouragement-first culture, private corrections, opt-in sharing).
- *🤖 AI Moment:* Unplugged "Are you smarter than a computer?" — students give a robot step-by-step instructions to make a sandwich; discover computers only do *exactly* what they're told. Plants the seed: AI is still just following instructions and patterns.
- *Homework (30 min):* "My AI scavenger hunt" — find and write down 3 places AI shows up at home (recommendations, voice assistants, autocomplete). One sentence each.

**Session 2 · Tue Jun 30 — Internet Safety, Responsible AI & Our First Algorithm**
- *Concept:* What an **algorithm** is (a precise sequence of steps); digital citizenship and internet safety.
- *🤖 AI Moment:* "Good robot / bad robot" — responsible AI basics: don't share private info, AI can be wrong, always check. Frame AI as a tool, not an authority.
- *Build:* Write a "morning routine" algorithm on paper, then act it out as a class — debug it when the steps fail.
- *Tier A:* Extra time on internet safety and "asking a grown-up." *Tier C:* Write an algorithm with a decision point (if it's raining...).
- *Homework:* Write a precise 6-step algorithm for a daily task (brushing teeth, feeding a pet). Bonus: add one "if."

**Session 3 · Wed Jul 1 — Scratch: Sequencing & Events**
- *Concept:* Sequencing and events in Scratch (green flag, sprites, motion). Computational thinking, not "mastering Scratch."
- *🤖 AI Moment:* "Sequence = recipe = training steps" — show that an AI is *trained* in a sequence of steps, just like scripts run in order.
- *Build:* Animate a sprite to move and react to the green flag and a key press.
- *Tier C challenge card:* Make the sprite respond to two different keys with different actions.
- *Homework:* Finish your animation so the sprite does at least 3 actions in order. Screenshot or save the project.

**Session 4 · Thu Jul 2 — Scratch: Loops**
- *Concept:* Loops (`repeat`, `forever`) — doing things many times without repeating yourself.
- *🤖 AI Moment:* "AI learns by repeating" — patterns get stronger the more examples it sees; loops are how machines practice.
- *Build:* Draw a shape/pattern with a `repeat` loop; make a sprite dance forever.
- *Tier A:* Use `repeat 4` to make a square (preview of `turtle` later). *Tier C challenge card:* Nested loops to draw a grid or spiral.
- *Homework:* Use a loop to make a repeating pattern or animation. Count how many lines of code your loop *saved*.

### Week 2 — Logic & First Shipped Project (Jul 6 – Jul 9)

**Session 5 · Mon Jul 6 — Scratch: Conditionals (if / if-else)**
- *Concept:* Conditionals — making decisions in code.
- *🤖 AI Moment:* "AI is a giant pile of if-then rules... sort of" — build a tiny "is it a cat?" decision tree on the board (has whiskers? says meow?). This is the seed of a **classifier**.
- *Build:* A sprite that says different things depending on where it is or what key is pressed.
- *Homework:* Add an if-else to a project so something different happens based on a condition.

**Session 6 · Tue Jul 7 — Scratch: Variables & Score-keeping**
- *Concept:* Variables — storing and changing values (score, lives, name).
- *🤖 AI Moment:* "Variables = memory" — connect to how AI keeps a 'score' (confidence) for each guess. Show a prediction with a confidence percentage.
- *Build:* Add a score variable to a clicker or catch game.
- *Tier C challenge card:* Add a high-score variable that persists during play.
- *Homework:* Add a working score or lives counter to your game.

**Session 7 · Wed Jul 8 — Scratch Mini-Project: "Ship It!" Build Day**
- *Goal:* **Every student ships one tiny interactive project** (one-screen game or animation) by end of session — a guaranteed win that directly addresses the 43% "fall behind" fear.
- *Build:* Choose a theme from interest hooks: a game, Minecraft-style, Mario/Hello-Kitty style.
- *🤖 AI Moment:* "Could an AI have made this for you? What would it need to know?" — intro to the idea that AI generates from examples.
- *Homework:* Polish your shipped project and write 2 sentences: what it does and one thing you'd add.

**Session 8 · Thu Jul 9 — The Bridge: Same Logic, Two Languages (Scratch → Python)**
- *Concept:* See the *same logic in text form*. Side-by-side: a Scratch script and its Python equivalent (`print`, a `for` loop).
- *🤖 AI Moment:* "AI reads and writes text too" — show how predicting the next word is like our `print` statements following a pattern.
- *Build:* Type your first Python: a `print` story and a `for` loop that counts. Tier A pairs up; Tier C writes a loop with a calculation.
- *Homework:* Type a 4-line Python "about me" using `print`. Run it and fix any error you see (intro to debugging).

### Week 3 — Python Foundations & MIDTERM (Jul 13 – Jul 16)

**Session 9 · Mon Jul 13 — Python: Variables, Input & Output**
- *Concept:* `input()`, variables, and string formatting in Python.
- *🤖 AI Moment:* "A chatbot is just input → process → output" — preview the chatbot we'll build later.
- *Build:* A **Mad Libs** generator — ask for words, print a silly story.
- *Tier C challenge card:* Add a number input and do math on it.
- *Homework:* Finish a Mad Libs with at least 4 inputs. Bonus: make it about a topic you love.

**Session 10 · Tue Jul 14 — Python: `turtle` Graphics + Loops (Midterm Review woven in)**
- *Concept:* `turtle` (standard library) — visible output bridges Scratch's visual world to Python text. Reinforce loops in Python.
- *🤖 AI Moment:* "Generative art = rules + randomness" — use `random` (stdlib) to make the turtle draw something different each run; connect to how generative AI mixes patterns + randomness.
- *Build:* Draw shapes and a colorful spiral with a `for` loop and `turtle`.
- *Review:* Quick whole-class recap game of all concepts so far (sequence, loop, conditional, variable) to prep for the midterm.
- *Homework:* Light review sheet (one page, bilingual) — predict-the-output puzzles. ~30 min, no new coding.

**Session 11 · Wed Jul 15 — 🧪 MIDTERM**
- *Format (age- and tier-appropriate, low-stress):*
  - **(1) Concept check** — short, mostly visual: match the term to its meaning (loop, conditional, variable, algorithm, training data); predict-the-output; "what is AI training data?" in the student's own words.
  - **(2) Mini-build** — a small guided task in Scratch *or* Python (student's choice; Tier A may use Scratch).
- *🤖 AI integration:* One section asks students to explain what AI is and give one example of AI being wrong (responsible-AI check).
- *Differentiation:* Tier A gets a shorter, more visual version; Tier C gets a Python-required mini-build. Graded on **effort and growth, not perfection** — sensitive students get private, encouraging feedback.
- *Homework:* None tonight (post-midterm breather) — optional: "teach a family member one thing you learned."

**Session 12 · Thu Jul 16 — Python: Conditionals & a Number-Guessing Game**
- *Concept:* `if/elif/else` in Python; comparison operators; `random`.
- *🤖 AI Moment:* "Higher / lower = the simplest search strategy an AI uses" — connect guessing to how machines narrow down answers efficiently.
- *Build:* Number-guessing game (computer picks, you guess, it says higher/lower).
- *Tier C challenge card:* Flip it — the computer guesses *your* number using a smart (binary-search) strategy.
- *Homework:* Finish the guessing game and add a guess counter (combine conditionals + variables).

### Week 4 — Python Games & Intro to AI (Jul 21 – Jul 23)

**Session 13 · Tue Jul 21 — Python: Functions (Reusable Blocks)**
- *Concept:* Defining and calling functions — packaging logic to reuse.
- *🤖 AI Moment:* "A function that always gives the same answer vs. an AI that improves" — rule-based vs. learned behavior, made concrete.

- *Ethics seed:* Give it only a few examples → see it fail → discuss why **more and better data matters**.

## Differentiation & Individual Support

- **Encouragement-first culture.** Praise effort and process publicly; give corrections privately. Students who are sensitive to criticism get gentle, private feedback only.
- **Focus supports.** Short tasks, the built-in movement break, fidget-friendly seating, and on-screen checklists for students who need focus support (those who need to stand periodically are accommodated).
- **Low-stakes sharing.** Default to pair-sharing; never cold-call. Presenting is always opt-in (only 21% enjoy it; 36% are "nervous but willing").
- **Mixed group work.** Short bursts of pair programming inside independent build time (75% want a blend). Pair distracted students with focused partners; gifted students always get challenge cards.
- **Advanced learners.** Always offer the challenge card and Tier C stretch goals so the 18% boredom risk is covered. Light intro to project tooling and GitHub for the most advanced students.
- **Youngest learners.** Grade 1–2 students start with internet safety, responsible AI, and confidence basics; buddy pairing; Scratch longer before Python.
