HOW TO USE THIS SCRIPT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Text in [BRACKETS] = instructor stage directions or timing cues
- "Quoted text" = suggested exact words to say aloud
- -> means a natural transition or follow-up thought
- [KEY IDEA] = deep conceptual explanation (say or adapt to the age of the room)
- [PAUSE] = student interaction moment -- stop and wait for responses
- [ROBOT] = the daily AI Moment tie-in (5-10 min, folded in, never a lecture)
- TIER A = Grades 1-3 scaffolding | TIER B = Grades 3-5 core | TIER C = Grades 6-9 stretch

PACING MAP (from the syllabus Daily Rhythm):
  Warm-up (unplugged) .............. 10 min   -> Slides 1-3
  Mini-lesson (one concept, live) .. 15-20 min -> Slides 4-8  [ROBOT folded in]
  Guided build (all together) ...... 20 min    -> Slides 9-11
  Movement / brain break ........... 5 min     -> Slide 12
  Open build + challenge cards ..... 30-40 min  -> Slides 13-14
  Share-out (opt-in, warm) ......... 10 min     -> Slide 15
  (Homework assigned at close) ..... -> Slide 16

  SECTION 1 (75 min): Warm-up(5) -> Mini-lesson+Robot(15) -> Guided build(20)
  -> Break(5) -> Open build(25) -> Quick share(5). Build only Script 1 (Game
  Start) and Script 2 (Scoring Points) together; treat the lives-system
  upgrade (Script 3, Slide 11) as a demo-and-preview instead of an all-build;
  homework unchanged.

  CALL-BACK: This is Session 6. Yesterday (Session 5) they learned
  CONDITIONALS (if / if-else) and built a sprite that reacted differently to
  a key press, plus met their first classifier: "is it a cat?" built out of
  if-then questions. Today's idea sits right next to that: a conditional
  makes a DECISION, but a decision about WHAT? Today we give the program
  something worth deciding about -- a NUMBER it remembers and changes over
  time. Reference yesterday's orange Control category constantly: today we
  meet a NEW orange-family category (Variables) that gives conditionals
  something real to check, like "if score = 10."

================================================================================
                     PART 1 -- WARM-UP (UNPLUGGED)  [~10 min]
                          No screens. Brains on.
================================================================================


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 1 -- TITLE & WELCOME  ("Scratch: Variables & Score-keeping")
[Duration: ~2 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the title slide as students settle. Keep laptops CLOSED -- this is a
no-screen warm-up. Write "How does a game REMEMBER your score?" on the board.
Energy: warm, a little mischievous -- today's idea is the reason games don't
"forget" anything.]

"Good morning, coders! Welcome to Session 6. Yesterday you taught your sprite
to make a DECISION -- if this, then that; if not, something else. Give me
back the two blocks we learned. If... and if-else. Beautiful."

"Here's the question on the board: how does a game REMEMBER your score? Think
about it -- your character can die, the screen can change, music can stop and
start, but somehow that little number in the corner keeps going up. It
doesn't forget. Where does that number LIVE?"

[KEY IDEA -- say slowly, this frames the whole lesson]:
"Today's answer is called a VARIABLE. Imagine you have a special locked box.
You can put any number inside it, change what's inside whenever you want, and
check what's inside at any time -- and the box remembers, even while
everything else in the game is happening. That box is what we build today."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 2 -- WARM-UP: THE LOCKED BOX -- THINK ABOUT IT, TURN & TALK
[Duration: ~5 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[No screens. This is the "Think About It" + "Turn & Talk" moment from the
plan. You can bring an actual small box or tin as a prop -- it makes the
metaphor land harder for younger tiers.]

"Here's the locked box again. I can put ANY number inside -- let's say I put
in a 5. [Write '5' on a sticky note, drop it in the box.] Now here's my
question: can I change what's inside without opening a new box?"

[PAUSE -- let them answer. Guide toward yes -- you just swap the note.]

"Right -- I don't need a brand-new box. I just change what's written on the
inside. Now: can I look inside and tell someone what's there, without taking
the note out?"

[PAUSE -- yes, you peek and report the number, the box stays intact.]

"Turn and talk to your neighbor for thirty seconds: name something in YOUR
life that is like this locked box -- something that stays the same 'box,' but
the NUMBER or VALUE inside it keeps changing. Think about money in a piggy
bank, your age, the temperature outside, a scoreboard."

[PAUSE -- 30-45 seconds of pair talk. Circulate and listen. Sample back 2-3:
'My piggy bank -- the box stays the same, the amount of money changes.' 'My
age -- I'm still ME, but the number changes every birthday.' 'The
scoreboard at a basketball game.']

[KEY IDEA]:
"Every one of those is the same shape: a labeled container that holds ONE
value at a time, and that value can change. In coding, we call that
container a VARIABLE. Say it with me: variable. One more time: variable. You
already understand it -- today we just give it a name and build one inside
Scratch."

[TIER A note: keep the box as a literal, physical prop -- open it, show the
sticky note, swap it. TIER C stretch: "Can you think of something that needs
MULTIPLE boxes at once, each tracking something different? Like a video game
tracking your score AND your lives AND your ammo, all at the same time?" Hold
that thought -- that's exactly where today's build is headed.]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 3 -- CORE CONCEPT: THE CENTRAL QUESTION (ARCADE / SCRATCH / AI)
[Duration: ~3 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the "Central Question" slide with its three examples: arcade game,
Scratch game, AI model. Still no student screens.]

"Let's lock in why this matters everywhere, not just in Scratch. Look at
these three examples on the slide."

"In an ARCADE GAME, the score updates every single second while you play --
it never resets itself by accident, even while everything else on screen is
exploding and flashing."

"In a SCRATCH GAME -- same idea. You are about to code that exact behavior
yourself, today."

"And in an AI MODEL -- believe it or not, an AI's 'confidence score,' the
number that says how sure it is about a guess, is stored the EXACT same way.
A variable. We'll come back to that in a few minutes."

[PAUSE -- ask: "What do all three of these have in common?" Let them answer.
Guide toward: a number that changes over time, but the program never loses
track of it.]

[KEY IDEA]:
"A variable is a labeled container that stores ONE value. You give it a
NAME -- like 'score' -- and it holds a number or text. You can read it,
change it, or reset it at any time while the program is running. That's the
whole definition, and it powers everything from your phone's step counter to
a self-driving car's speed reading to an AI's confidence in its own guess."


================================================================================
              PART 2 -- MINI-LESSON (ONE CONCEPT, LIVE)  [~15-20 min]
                    Teacher demos on the big screen only.
================================================================================


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 4 -- BIG IDEA #1: A VARIABLE IS A LABELED CONTAINER
[Duration: ~4 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Now open Scratch on the projector -- YOUR screen only. Do not click into the
Variables category yet -- first re-anchor the definition with the visual
"Variable Name / Value Inside" card from the slide.]

"Here is our definition, straight from the slide: a variable is a labeled
container that stores one value."

[Point to the example card: Variable Name = score, Value Inside = 0.]

"See the two halves? The NAME -- 'score' -- never changes. It's the label on
the box. The VALUE -- right now, 0 -- is what's INSIDE the box, and that's
the part that changes constantly while your game runs."

[KEY IDEA]:
"There are exactly three things you can DO with a variable, and I want you
to lock these into memory right now:"

"One -- SET it. 'Set score to 0' -- this REPLACES whatever was inside with a
brand-new value, no matter what was there before."

"Two -- CHANGE it. 'Change score by 1' -- this ADDS to whatever is already
inside. It doesn't erase the old value, it builds on top of it."

"Three -- CHECK it. 'If score = 10, then...' -- this READS the current value
to make a decision. Sound familiar? That's yesterday's conditional, now
checking a variable instead of a key press!"

[PAUSE -- ask: "Which of those three -- set, change, or check -- do you think
happens ONCE, at the very start of a game, and which happens OVER AND OVER
while you play?" Let them answer. Guide toward: set usually happens once at
the start; change and check happen repeatedly.]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 5 -- BIG IDEA #2: MAKING A VARIABLE + THE FOUR KEY BLOCKS
[Duration: ~5 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Click into the ORANGE Variables category live on the projector. Students
should notice it sits right next to yesterday's orange Control category --
Scratch groups its "thinking and remembering" blocks in warm colors.]

"To make a variable, here's the exact path: click the orange 'Variables'
category, click 'Make a Variable,' type a name -- like 'score' -- and click
OK."

[Demo it live, narrating every click: category, button, name box, OK. Watch
the new orange blocks appear in the palette, plus the little checkbox monitor
appear on the Stage.]

"Look what just happened in TWO places at once. First, in the palette, you
now have a whole new set of orange blocks, custom-made with YOUR variable's
name already built in. Second, up on the Stage, a little scoreboard just
appeared automatically, already showing 0."

"There are four key variable blocks I want you to recognize on sight:"

"Block 1 -- 'set score to 0' -- the REPLACE block."
"Block 2 -- 'change score by 1' -- the ADD-ON-TOP block."
"Block 3 -- the plain orange OVAL labeled 'score' by itself -- this is a
REPORTER. It doesn't do an action -- it just holds and reports the current
number."
"Block 4 -- 'show variable score' / 'hide variable score' -- these control
whether the scoreboard is visible on Stage at all."

[Demo: drag the plain orange 'score' oval into a 'say' block's slot.]

[KEY IDEA]:
"What does that orange oval actually DO when you drag it somewhere else?
When you drag the orange 'score' oval into another block, it READS the
current value -- like peeking inside the box without opening it up or
changing anything. It's the 'check' action from a minute ago, in block
form."

[TIER C stretch: "Notice the oval and the 'set'/'change' blocks all say
'score' -- but they behave completely differently. One replaces, one adds,
one just peeks. Same NAME, three different JOBS."]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 6 -- BIG IDEA #3: THE VARIABLE BADGE ON THE STAGE
[Duration: ~3 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the Stage with the score monitor visible. Right-click it live on the
projector to show the display-mode menu.]

"See that little monitor sitting on the Stage? That's Scratch showing you
the CURRENT value of your variable, live, updating instantly the moment it
changes. You can even drag it anywhere you want on the Stage -- top corner,
bottom, wherever fits your game."

[Demo: right-click the monitor, show the three display modes -- normal
readout, large readout, slider.]

"Right-click it and you get THREE display modes. Normal shows the label and
number together. Large readout blows the number up huge -- great for a final
score screen. Slider turns it into a draggable bar -- useful for things like
volume or difficulty, not so much for score, but good to know it exists."

[PAUSE -- ask: "Why do you think it's useful that the Stage shows this
automatically, instead of you having to build your own scoreboard display
from scratch?" Let them answer. Guide toward: it saves you from building your
own display logic -- Scratch does it for free the moment you check the
box.]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 7 -- [ROBOT] AI MOMENT: VARIABLES = MEMORY
[Duration: ~5-6 minutes -- this is the daily AI tie-in, folded in]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the "AI Moment - Variables = Memory" slide, side by side: your
Scratch score variable vs. an AI model identifying a cat photo. Call back to
yesterday's AI moment -- the "is it a cat?" decision tree.]

"Now my favorite part of every day -- how does today's idea connect to AI?"

"Yesterday we built a tiny classifier on the board -- a chain of if-then
questions that decided 'cat or not cat.' Here's the question I left hanging:
when that classifier finally answers, how SURE is it?"

[Point to the slide's AI model example.]

"When a real AI model looks at a photo and guesses 'cat,' it doesn't just say
yes or no -- it stores a NUMBER alongside that guess, called a CONFIDENCE
SCORE. Something like 94% sure it's a cat. That number lives... in a
variable. The exact same kind of container you just built."

[KEY IDEA]:
"Think about the parallel very literally. YOUR Scratch game: score starts at
0, and it grows with each good action -- each click, each catch. An AI
model: its confidence starts at some number, and it UPDATES as it processes
more of the image, more of the pattern, refining its guess. Same shape.
Set a starting value. Change it as new information comes in. Check it to
decide what to do next."

[PAUSE -- ask: "If an AI's confidence score variable said only 51% sure it's
a cat, instead of 94%, what does that tell you?" Let them answer. Guide
toward: the AI is barely more confident than a coin flip -- it's uncertain,
and maybe shouldn't be trusted on that one.]

"That's exactly why variables matter so much in AI -- they're not just
keeping score in a game. In AI, every time a model processes an image, it's
updating dozens, sometimes MILLIONS, of number-containers called weights --
which are really just variables, at a much bigger scale. Today's tiny
'score' variable is the same idea an entire AI system is built on top of."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 8 -- WATCH: VIDEO 1 & VIDEO 2 -- SEEING VARIABLES LIVE
[Duration: ~4 minutes video + 2 minutes talk]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Play Video 1 on the projector. Prime them with focus questions before you
press play, matching the on-screen prompts.]

"Before I hit play on Video 1 -- watch for TWO exact moments: when does
'score' get set to 0, and when does 'lives' go down? Keep both in your
head."

[Play Video 1, ~2-3 minutes.]

"Pause and think with me: why does the teacher in the video add a 'wait 1
second' block right after lives changes? What problem is that solving?"

[PAUSE -- let them guess. Hold the full answer for the Guided Build, Slide
11 -- just plant the question here: without a pause, something bad happens
FAST.]

"One more question from the video: why do you think it uses 'change by -1'
instead of some totally separate block just for losing a life?"

[PAUSE -- guide toward: change works for negative numbers too -- it's the
exact same tool, just subtracting instead of adding.]

[Play Video 2, ~2 minutes -- the 3-step recipe.]

"Video 2 showed the exact 3-step recipe we're about to build together. Say
it back to me after I say it once: when green flag clicked... set score to
0... when sprite clicked, change score by 1. Now you say it."

[PAUSE -- let the room say the recipe out loud together, even messily.
Celebrate it. This chant is the whole guided build in one sentence.]


================================================================================
                 PART 3 -- GUIDED BUILD (ALL TOGETHER)  [~20 min]
                  Everyone builds the SAME thing, step by step.
================================================================================


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 9 -- GUIDED BUILD SETUP + SCRIPT 1: GAME START (set score to 0)
[Duration: ~7 minutes -- go slowly, narrate every drag]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Students open Scratch NOW. Walk the room -- confirm everyone has a sprite
and has already made a variable named 'score' (from Slide 5) before
continuing. Buddy-pair Tier A / Grade 1-2 kids.]

"Screens ON. Make sure you already have your 'score' variable made from
earlier -- orange Variables category, 'Make a Variable,' name it score. Thumbs
up when you see the scoreboard on your Stage. [Wait for all thumbs.]"

"Here's what every one of us will have by the end of this guided build: a
sprite whose score starts at 0 every single time you press green flag, and
climbs by 1 every time you click it. We build it together, step by step --
do exactly what I do, no more, no less."

"SCRIPT 1 -- GAME START. This script runs ONCE, the instant the game
starts. Its whole job is to make sure score always begins at zero -- not at
whatever number it was last time you played."

"Step 1a: drag a 'when green flag clicked' hat block onto an empty spot.
[Wait.]"

"Step 1b: from orange Variables, grab 'set [score] to [0]' and snap it
directly underneath. [Wait -- check every screen.]"

"Read it back with me: 'when green flag clicked... set score to 0.' Click
the green flag right now and watch your scoreboard. What does it say?"

[PAUSE -- answer: 0. Celebrate.]

[KEY IDEA -- "Why set to 0?"]:
"Here's the trap I want you to fall into on purpose, just once, so you never
forget the lesson. Click the green flag again a few times, but this time,
BEFORE you rebuild Script 1, imagine you had SKIPPED it entirely. Without
that 'set score to 0' block, your score would start wherever it happened to
end last time you played -- 7, 12, whatever was left over. A fresh game
would feel broken because it would remember the WRONG thing. Setting the
starting value is not optional decoration -- it's how you guarantee every
new game actually starts new."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 10 -- GUIDED BUILD SCRIPT 2: SCORING POINTS (change score by 1)
[Duration: ~7 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the "Scoring Points" slide. Build the second script directly next
to the first, still on the projector, one block at a time.]

"SCRIPT 2 -- SCORING POINTS. This script runs EVERY SINGLE TIME the sprite
is clicked -- not once, over and over, however many times you click."

"Step 2a: drag a 'when this sprite clicked' hat block onto a fresh, empty
spot -- separate from Script 1. [Wait.]"

"Step 2b: from orange Variables, grab 'change [score] by [1]' and snap it
underneath. [Wait -- check every screen.]"

"Read it back with me: 'when this sprite clicked... change score by 1.'
Click the green flag to reset to 0, then click your sprite five times fast.
Watch the scoreboard."

[PAUSE -- let them test it, click repeatedly, laugh at the number climbing.
Have them click the STAGE instead of the sprite -- nothing happens, which is
exactly correct, because only the sprite's click event fires this script.]

[KEY IDEA -- "Why change by 1, not set to 1?"]:
"Here's a question worth sitting with: what would happen if I used 'set
score to 1' every time you clicked, instead of 'change score by 1'? Try it
right now -- swap the block and click five times."

[PAUSE -- let them discover: the score freezes at 1 forever, because 'set'
REPLACES the value with 1 every single time, no matter what was there
before. 'Change' ADDS to whatever is currently inside the box. Swap the
block back to 'change' once they've seen the bug live.]

"'Change by' adds to the current value. 'Set to 1' would just stay stuck at
1 forever -- it doesn't know or care what was there a second ago. That one
word -- SET versus CHANGE -- is the single most common bug in every scoring
system you will ever build, so burn it into memory right now."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 11 -- GUIDED BUILD LEVEL UP: THE if BLOCK RETURNS + LIVES SYSTEM
[Duration: ~6 minutes -- SECTION 1 demos this instead of all-build]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the "Level Up" slide. This is where yesterday's conditional and
today's variable finally meet in one block.]

"Quick callback to yesterday: what block did we learn that checks something
and decides what to do?" [PAUSE -- answer: if / if-else.] "Today, that block
finally has something REAL and NUMERIC to check."

"Add an 'if' block: from Control, grab 'if <> then.' In the hexagon slot,
from Operators, drag an equals block, put the orange 'score' oval on the
left side, and type 10 on the right -- 'score = 10.' Inside the mouth, from
Looks, add 'say [You Win!] for 2 seconds.'"

"Snap this if-block right after your 'change score by 1' block, still inside
Script 2. Click the sprite ten times and watch."

[PAUSE -- celebrate the "You Win!" popping up at exactly 10. This is the
first time a conditional has checked a VARIABLE instead of a key or a
sensor -- name that out loud.]

[KEY IDEA]:
"Yesterday your if-block checked 'is the spacebar pressed?' -- a sensor.
Today it checks 'is score equal to 10?' -- a variable. Same if-block,
completely new kind of question, because now your program has a NUMBER
worth asking about."

"Now let's add SCRIPT 3 -- THE LIVES SYSTEM, still on the projector. Make a
SECOND variable called 'lives.' Under green flag, set lives to 3. Then build
a script: when sprite touches a bad object, or when a key is pressed for
this demo, do 'change lives by -1,' then IMMEDIATELY add a 'wait 1 second'
block right after it."

[Demo removing the wait block on purpose, then triggering the lose-a-life
event rapidly.]

"Watch what happens WITHOUT the wait block -- lives drops to 0 in what feels
like milliseconds, way faster than you meant, because the check keeps firing
over and over inside that loop before you've even let go. Now watch WITH the
wait block back in."

[PAUSE -- let them see the difference live. This answers the question
planted back at Slide 8.]

[KEY IDEA -- "Why wait 1 second is crucial"]:
"The 'wait 1 second' block is not decoration -- it's a SPEED LIMIT.
Without it, a loop can check and fire a change block dozens of times before
you've even reacted, because computers run WAY faster than human reflexes.
The wait block forces a pause between each loss, so 'change lives by -1'
happens once per real event, not fifty times per second."

"THREE TIERS, SAME CONCEPT, going into Open Build:"
"TIER A -- add a 'score' variable only. Make sure it resets to 0 and climbs
by 1 on click. That's a complete, real win."
"TIER B -- add BOTH 'score' (climbs) and 'lives' (starts at 3, drops on
collision, with the wait block in place)."
"TIER C CHALLENGE -- add a HIGH-SCORE variable: after the round ends, if
score is greater than highScore, update highScore to match score. Show the
Tier C code on the slide: 'if score > highScore then set highScore to
score.'"

[TIER C stretch, explain the high-score pattern aloud]: "Notice highScore
doesn't change every round -- it only changes when the new score BEATS the
old record. That's a variable that remembers the BEST value it has ever
seen, not just the most recent one. Hold onto that idea -- it's exactly how
we're about to describe AI in tonight's homework."

"QUICK BUG CHECK before we move on: press green flag. Does lives start at
3? Does score start at 0? If either one is wrong, you are missing a 'set'
block under your green flag hat -- go check right now."

[SECTION 1 (75 min): demo Script 3 (lives + wait block) on the projector
only; let students build through Script 1 and Script 2 themselves, then
protect time for Open Build. Homework is unchanged.]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 12 -- BRAIN BREAK
[Duration: ~5 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the calm break slide. This is an essential focus reset -- honor it
fully, do not skip it.]

"Everybody stand up -- variables need a break too! Arms up, big stretch as
high as you can reach. Hold it... and release."

"Now roll your neck slowly, one direction, then the other. Nice and easy."

"Three deep breaths with me. In... and out. In... and out. One more -- in...
and out."

"Tiny thing to dream about while we breathe, no pressure: when we come back,
YOUR sprite gets to decide what its score means -- points for catching
something, points for surviving, points for anything you invent. Open Build
starts in five minutes. Your brain will thank you for this pause."

[Let them move, drink water, breathe. Bring them back gently and seated.]


================================================================================
             PART 5 -- OPEN BUILD + CHALLENGE CARDS  [~30-40 min]
              Apply the concept to your OWN project. Explore & stretch.
================================================================================


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 13 -- OPEN BUILD LAUNCH: YOUR BUILD GOAL BY TIER
[Duration: ~2 minutes launch, then circulate for the rest of the block]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the tiered "Your build goal" slide. Send students off with a clear,
differentiated target, then spend the block circulating, not lecturing.]

"Here is your build goal for the next 30 to 40 minutes, and it depends on
your tier -- remember, everyone is working on the SAME underlying idea,
variables, just at your own depth."

"TIER A -- add a working 'score' variable to your own project. Make it reset
at green flag and climb by 1 on a click or key press."

"TIER B -- add BOTH score and lives to your own project, fully working:
score climbs, lives starts at 3 and drops correctly, with a wait block
protecting it."

"TIER C -- add score, lives, AND a high-score variable that only updates
when you beat your previous best. Bonus: try the 'if score = 10, say You
Win' pattern with a number that makes sense for YOUR game."

[KEY IDEA -- the driving question, keep repeating it as you circulate]:
"Keep asking yourself as you build: what CHANGES in my game when something
happens? That changing thing is a variable. Every time you find yourself
wanting to remember a number across time -- points, health, ammo, a timer --
that's your cue to reach for a variable, not a fixed number typed into a
block."

"Quick reminder while you work: in AI, every time a model processes an
image, it updates dozens of 'score' variables called weights. You are
literally practicing the same pattern real AI systems run on, just at kid
scale."

[Circulate the room during this block. Use the Debugging card below reactively
whenever a bug appears -- don't lecture it up front.]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 14 -- [DEBUGGING] COMMON VARIABLE BUGS -- USE REACTIVELY DURING OPEN BUILD
[Duration: folded into circulation, ~1 minute per bug as it comes up]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Do NOT teach this as a block up front -- walk the room and diagnose live,
matching whichever bug a student actually hits. Keep this slide open on your
own laptop as a quick-reference while you circulate.]

"BUG #1 -- Score Starts at Old Number. If your score doesn't start at 0,
you're missing 'set score to 0' under 'when green flag clicked.' Always add
it -- every single project, every single time."

"BUG #2 -- Score Never Changes. If clicking does nothing, check whether you
accidentally used 'set score to 1' instead of 'change score by 1.' Set
replaces; change adds. Swap it."

"BUG #3 -- Lives Vanishes Instantly. If lives drops to 0 the moment you
touch something, add a 'wait 1 second' block right after 'change lives by
-1.' Without it, the check fires dozens of times before you can react."

"BUG #4 -- Score Invisible on Stage. If you don't see your scoreboard at
all, check the little checkbox next to 'score' in the Variables palette, or
add a 'show variable score' block."

[Use this as a checklist while circulating -- point at the exact bug number
when you spot it on a student's screen: "That's Bug 2 -- come look at this
with me."]


================================================================================
                   PART 6 -- SHARE-OUT (OPT-IN, WARM)  [~10 min]
================================================================================


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 15 -- SHARE-OUT: SHOW & TELL, EXPLAIN, ONE THING NEXT
[Duration: ~10 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the share-out slide. Keep this low-stakes and opt-in -- pair demo
is always the default, whole-group volunteers are a bonus, never a
requirement.]

"Turn to the person next to you and demonstrate your score variable.
Remember -- sharing is ALWAYS optional. Pair demo is our default; volunteering
to show the whole group is a nice bonus, never a requirement."

"SHOW & TELL prompt: 'Here's my score variable -- watch it go up when I
click!' Let your partner actually see the number move."

"EXPLAIN prompt: ask your partner, 'What does change score by 1 actually do
inside the computer?' -- and have them answer in their own words, not just
repeat the block's name."

"ONE THING NEXT prompt: finish with, 'Tomorrow I want to add...' -- name one
small thing you'd build next."

[PAUSE -- circulate during pair-shares, listening for strong explanations.
Invite 1-2 volunteers to show the whole room if energy allows -- never
cold-call.]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 16 -- WRAP-UP + HOMEWORK ("Today's session complete! / See you tomorrow!")
[Duration: ~3 minutes wrap + 1 minute to assign homework]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the recap/homework slide. Deliver with genuine warmth. Session 7
(Ship It! Build Day) is the very next class day -- this is the last building
block before every student ships a finished project.]

"Look at what you did today. Three big wins."

"YOU LEARNED: a variable is a named container. It stores one value that you
can read, change, or reset anytime your program runs."

================================================================================
APPENDIX A -- COMMON PITFALLS & HOW TO HANDLE THEM
================================================================================

PITFALL: Score doesn't reset between plays -- keeps climbing from last game.
  Handle: "Is 'set score to 0' attached under 'when green flag clicked'?
          Without it, your box just keeps whatever number was left inside
          from last time. Add the set block, and always put it first."

PITFALL: Student uses 'set score to 1' repeatedly instead of 'change score
by 1,' and the score gets stuck.
  Handle: "Set REPLACES the box's contents no matter what was there before.
          Change ADDS to whatever's already inside. If you want it to
          climb, you need change, not set."

PITFALL: Lives drops from 3 to 0 almost instantly on one touch.
  Handle: "Add a 'wait 1 second' block right after 'change lives by -1.'
          Without it, the check can fire dozens of times in under a second
          -- way faster than you can react -- and drain all your lives in
          one touch."

PITFALL: The scoreboard doesn't appear on the Stage at all.
  Handle: "Check the little checkbox next to your variable's name in the
          Variables palette -- unchecking it hides the monitor. You can
          also add a 'show variable score' block to force it visible."

PITFALL: Student drags the round 'score' oval into an 'if' block's hexagon
slot directly, and it won't fit.
  Handle: "That's yesterday's rule again -- the hexagon slot only accepts
          true/false blocks. The 'score' oval holds a NUMBER, not a
          true/false answer, so it needs to sit INSIDE an equals or
          greater-than block from Operators first -- then THAT block, which
          is a hexagon, goes into the if-slot."

PITFALL: High-score variable updates every single round, even when the new
score is lower than the old record.
  Handle: "Check your if-block's condition -- it should say 'score >
          highScore,' not just run unconditionally. The whole point of a
          high score is that it only updates when the NEW value actually
          beats the OLD one."

PITFALL: Young student (Grade 1-2) overwhelmed by two variables (score and
lives) at once.
  Handle: Narrow the world. "Today we only need ONE box: score. Set it to
          0, change it by 1 on click. That's the whole build." Buddy them
          and celebrate the first climbing number.

PITFALL: Fast Tier-C student finishes score + lives + high score in minutes
and is bored.
  Handle: Hand the "if score = 10, say You Win" challenge immediately, or
          invite them to add a second win condition, a countdown timer
          variable, or mentor a younger buddy through Script 1 and 2.

PITFALL: "Why do I need TWO scripts instead of putting everything in one?"
  Handle: "Let's test it. Script 1 needs to run exactly ONCE, at the very
          start. Script 2 needs to run MANY times, on every click. If you
          crammed both into one script under green flag, the scoring part
          would only ever run once too -- you need two separate event hats
          because they run on two separate triggers."
