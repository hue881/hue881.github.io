# 7-Week AI & Coding Curriculum with Scratch Game Development
## For 6th Grade Students | 4 Lessons per Week | 90 Minutes per Lesson

---

## Course Overview

This curriculum integrates foundational concepts in artificial intelligence, machine learning, AI ethics, and practical coding skills using Scratch. Students will develop a complete video game incorporating AI mechanics and apply ethical reasoning throughout the development process. The course bridges computational thinking with real-world AI applications, preparing students to understand and create with intelligent systems.

**Total Duration:** 7 weeks | 28 lessons (100 minutes each) | 2,800 minutes of instruction

---

## Learning Objectives (Course-Wide)

By the end of this course, students will be able to:
- Understand foundational concepts of machine learning and artificial intelligence
- Recognize ethical implications of AI systems in society
- Design and code interactive games using Scratch with AI mechanics
- Apply problem-solving and computational thinking to real-world scenarios
- Collaborate effectively in team-based game development projects
- Evaluate AI systems for bias, fairness, and impact

---

## Course Philosophy & Scaffolding

This curriculum scaffolds from **foundational understanding** (Week 1: AI/ML basics) through **applied practice** (Weeks 3-7: game development with AI), ensuring students develop conceptual knowledge before implementation. Each week builds on the previous, with the final project synthesizing all learned concepts into a publishable game product.

# WEEK 1: Foundations of AI, Machine Learning & Computational Thinking

## Overview
Week 1 establishes the conceptual foundation for AI and machine learning, introduces Scratch as a development environment, and begins unpacking AI ethics. Students learn through discussion, exploration, and initial hands-on activities.

### **LESSON 1.1: What is Artificial Intelligence? (Monday)**

**Duration:** 100 minutes

#### Learning Objectives
- Define AI and distinguish it from other forms of technology
- Identify examples of AI in everyday life
- Understand the difference between narrow AI and general AI
- Develop curiosity about AI's role in society

#### Materials Required
- Smartboard/Projector & laptop connection
- Student laptops/Chromebooks (1 per student)
- Index cards or sticky notes
- Whiteboard/markers
- YouTube access (for AI examples video)

#### Lesson Structure

**Opening (10 minutes): "The AI Around Us" Hook**
- Show a short video compilation (3–4 minutes) of AI in action: TikTok recommendations, voice assistants, facial recognition, game NPCs
- Pose question: "How many of these have you used in the last week?"
- Discuss student responses (popcorn style)

**Instruction (30 minutes): Defining AI**
1. **What Makes Something "Intelligent"?** (10 min)
   - Brain storm as a class: What does "thinking" mean? What can computers do that seems intelligent?
   - Write student ideas on whiteboard, cluster them into categories (learning, deciding, predicting, adapting)

2. **Introducing AI Formally** (10 min)
   - Explain: AI = systems designed to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence
   - Key examples: pattern recognition, learning from experience, making decisions
   - Distinguish between: narrow AI (designed for one task, like chess) vs. general AI (hypothetical: can do anything humans do)
   - Real-world clarification: All AI we have today is narrow AI

3. **How Does AI Actually Work?** (10 min)
   - Simplified explanation: AI learns from examples (data)
   - Example: Show how Netflix learns your preferences over time
   - Formula on board: **Data + Learning Algorithm → AI System → Predictions/Decisions**

**Guided Practice (35 minutes): AI Scavenger Hunt**
1. Divide class into 4 groups (5–6 students each)
2. Assign each group one category: Games, Entertainment, Communication, Home/School
3. On laptops, students explore and document AI systems in their category (10 minutes research)
4. Groups create a visual poster (digital or paper sketch) showing: AI name, how it works simply, why it's helpful (10 minutes)
5. Each group presents their findings (15 minutes: 3–4 min per group)

**Discussion & Closing (15 minutes): AI Ethics Introduction**
- Reflection: "Is all AI helpful?"
- Mini-scenarios presented:
  - A school uses an AI to predict which students might drop out. Good or problematic? Why?
  - A game uses AI to track your playing patterns and make the game harder if you're winning easily. Fair? 
  - A social media AI shows you content you like, but you only see things from one side of a debate. Problem?
- Students vote (agree/disagree) and explain reasoning
- Introduce the term "AI Ethics": rules for building AI that's fair, honest, and helpful
- Bridge: "We'll explore ethics throughout this course as we build AI systems ourselves"

#### Assessment
- Observation of participation in discussion and group work
- Quality of AI examples documented (accuracy, clarity)
- Depth of reasoning in ethics scenarios

#### Exit Ticket (5 minutes)
"On a scale of 1–5, how much do you think AI affects your daily life? Name one AI system you'll pay attention to this week."

#### Homework / Out-of-Class Extension
- Look for 3 examples of AI used in their home or community (describe briefly)
- Watch a 5-minute video on "How Machine Learning Works" (curated link provided)

### **LESSON 1.2: Machine Learning Fundamentals (Tuesday)**

**Duration:** 100 minutes

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand the core concept of machine learning (learning from data)
- Recognize the three main types of machine learning: supervised, unsupervised, reinforcement
- Identify real-world applications of each type
- Begin to see connections between learning and coding

#### Materials Required
- Smartboard/Projector
- Student laptops/Chromebooks
- Printable worksheets: "ML Types Matching Activity"
- Index cards (for sorting activity)
- Sample dataset images (flowers, numbers, etc.)
- Optional: Simple ML demo tool (Google's Teachable Machine)

#### Lesson Structure

**Opening (10 minutes): Training a Brain**
- Interactive demo: "Teach the AI" challenge
  - Show images of cats vs. dogs briefly, then have students predict if a new image is a cat or dog
  - Do this 5 times, then show students the "rule" they were learning (silhouette shape, ear position, etc.)
  - Connection: "You just learned like a machine learning algorithm!"

**Instruction (25 minutes): The Three Types of Machine Learning**

1. **Supervised Learning** (8 min)
   - Definition: Learning from labeled examples ("Here's a cat. Here's a dog. Now identify this.")
   - Analogy: "Like learning with a teacher who gives you the right answer"
   - Real examples:
     - Email spam filters (trained on examples of spam and non-spam)
     - Medical diagnosis AI (trained on images of healthy vs. diseased tissue)
     - Handwriting recognition (trained on thousands of written letters)
   - Draw on board: labeled examples → algorithm → predictions

2. **Unsupervised Learning** (8 min)
   - Definition: Finding patterns in unlabeled data ("Figure out what's similar on your own")
   - Analogy: "Like finding your own rules without a teacher"
   - Real examples:
     - Netflix grouping similar movies (without being told what makes them similar)
     - Spotify creating playlists by discovering which songs people listen to together
     - Recommendation algorithms
   - Key insight: The AI finds patterns you might not have thought of

3. **Reinforcement Learning** (9 min)
   - Definition: Learning through trial, error, and rewards ("Get better by practicing and getting feedback")
   - Analogy: "Like training a dog with treats for good behavior"
   - Real examples:
     - Game AI learning to play (AlphaGo, chess computers)
     - Robot learning to walk
     - Recommendation systems that learn what makes you happy
   - Connection to games: Many game AIs use this type of learning!

**Guided Practice (45 minutes): ML Type Sorting & Google Teachable Machine**

**Activity 1: Sorting Challenge (20 minutes)**
1. Display 12 real-world AI scenario cards (printed or on screen)
   - Examples: 
     - Predicting house prices based on size and location
     - Grouping Netflix users by watching behavior
     - AlphaGo learning to beat humans at Go
     - Spotify radio discovering new songs
     - Photo tagging friends on Instagram
     - Medical imaging diagnosing cancer
     - etc.
2. Students sort cards into three categories: Supervised, Unsupervised, Reinforcement
3. Discuss as a class, revealing correct answers and explaining why each fits
4. Students take notes on why each type is useful

**Activity 2: Teachable Machine Exploration (25 minutes)**
1. Project Google's Teachable Machine (no account needed: https://teachablemachine.withgoogle.com/)
2. Live demo with class: Train an image classifier in real-time
   - Teacher holds up three different objects/poses
   - "Teach" the AI by holding each in front of the camera for 3–5 seconds (supervised learning demo)
   - AI instantly learns to recognize the three objects/poses
   - Show the AI's "confidence" (probability) for each prediction
3. Pair students up; each pair trains their own classifier:
   - Possible themes: hand gestures (thumbs up/down/rock), facial expressions, objects
   - 2 minutes to train, 5 minutes to test with others
   - Discuss: How does accuracy improve with more training examples?

**Discussion & Closing (10 minutes): Connecting to Code & Games**
- "All of this—supervised, unsupervised, reinforcement learning—can be built into games"
- Example: Game NPC learning player behavior and adapting difficulty
- Bridge to next weeks: "In Scratch, we'll code game AI using these principles"
- Question: "In the game you build, what could you teach your AI to do?"

#### Assessment
- Correct categorization of ML scenario cards (with reasoning)
- Observation of Teachable Machine exploration (engagement, understanding)
- Exit ticket responses

#### Exit Ticket (5 minutes)
"Which type of machine learning would you use to build a game NPC that gets smarter the longer you play? Why?"

#### Homework / Out-of-Class Extension
- Play 2–3 simple games online (provided links) and notice how the AI behaves
- Journal: "How do you think the game AI learned to do that?"
- Optional: Spend 10 minutes with Teachable Machine at home (personal device)

### **LESSON 1.3: AI Ethics & Fairness (Wednesday)**

**Duration:** 100 minutes

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand the core principles of AI ethics: fairness, transparency, accountability, privacy
- Recognize bias in AI systems
- Explore the impact of flawed AI on society
- Develop frameworks for ethical decision-making in AI design

#### Materials Required
- Smartboard/Projector
- Student laptops/Chromebooks
- Printable case study handouts (3 scenarios)
- Voting cards/sticky notes
- Video clip: "Bias in AI" (4–5 minutes, age-appropriate)
- Discussion facilitation guide

#### Lesson Structure

**Opening (10 minutes): The Consequences of AI**
- Thought experiment: "Imagine an AI decides whether students get into advanced classes. What could go wrong?"
- Popcorn responses; write key concerns on board
- Reveal real story: Several schools/employers have faced lawsuits because their AI discriminated against protected groups
- Today's question: "How do we make sure AI is fair and ethical?"

**Instruction (25 minutes): Core Principles of AI Ethics**

1. **Fairness** (7 min)
   - Definition: AI systems shouldn't discriminate or treat groups unfairly
   - Problem: If training data reflects historical bias, AI learns and perpetuates it
   - Example: Resume-screening AI rejecting women's applications because historical data showed men in tech
   - Real consequence: Company had to remove the system
   - Key question: "How do we teach an AI to be fair?"

2. **Transparency** (6 min)
   - Definition: People should understand how AI makes decisions
   - Problem: "Black box" systems make decisions we can't explain
   - Example: A loan officer can tell you why they rejected your loan, but an AI just says "denied"
   - Ethical standard: Users deserve to know why an AI made a decision
   - Real law: EU's GDPR gives people the right to explanation

3. **Accountability** (6 min)
   - Definition: Someone is responsible when AI causes harm
   - Problem: Who's at fault? The coder? The company? The AI itself?
   - Example: A self-driving car hits a pedestrian—who's responsible?
   - Ethical standard: Designers and companies must take responsibility for AI impacts
   - Connection: "When we build games, we're responsible for how they affect players"

4. **Privacy** (6 min)
   - Definition: AI should respect personal information
   - Problem: AI systems often collect and use data without people knowing
   - Example: Your gaming data being sold to marketers without permission
   - Ethical standard: Informed consent—people should choose what data is used
   - Real law: Schools have rules about student data privacy

**Video & Discussion (15 minutes): Real Cases of AI Bias**
- Watch: "Bias in AI" video (curated, age-appropriate, 4–5 min)
  - Examples: facial recognition failing for darker skin tones, recruiting AI discriminating against women, predictive policing targeting certain neighborhoods
- Pause and discuss after each case:
  - "What went wrong?"
  - "Who was harmed?"
  - "How could it have been prevented?"
- Write ethical lessons on board

**Guided Practice (40 minutes): Case Study & Design Challenge**

**Case Study Analysis (20 minutes):**
1. Divide class into 3 groups; each group gets one scenario (printed handout):
   
   **Scenario A: The Game Difficulty AI**
   - A game company creates an AI that learns your skill level and adjusts difficulty automatically
   - Problem: The AI is much better at learning from boys' gameplay patterns; it misjudges girls' skill levels and gives them levels that are too hard or too easy
   - Questions: Why did this happen? Who is harmed? How do we fix it?
   
   **Scenario B: The Recommendation Algorithm**
   - An educational game recommends levels and hints based on player data
   - Problem: The algorithm learned from past data showing that students from certain neighborhoods spent more on in-game purchases; now it recommends expensive items more to students from those areas
   - Questions: Is this discrimination? What if those students can't afford it? How do we fix it?
   
   **Scenario C: The NPC Personality**
   - A game's AI characters "learn" from player interactions and develop personality quirks
   - Problem: The AI has been trained primarily on dialogue from male characters and now generates responses that are hostile toward players who use they/them pronouns
   - Questions: How did this bias enter the code? What's the impact? How do we fix it?

2. Groups analyze and answer guided questions on worksheet (10 minutes)
3. Groups present findings to class (1 minute per group; 3 minutes total)

**Design Challenge (20 minutes): "Build Fair AI"**
1. Introduce a simple game design: A platformer that suggests which level a new player should start at
2. Challenge: Design how the AI should decide what difficulty is "fair"
   - What data should it use?
   - What data should it NOT use?
   - How would you test for bias?
3. Each group brainstorms on large paper/whiteboard (10 minutes)
4. Gallery walk and peer feedback (5 minutes)
5. Discuss: "These principles apply to the games you'll build in Weeks 3–7!"

**Closing (10 minutes): Ethical Commitment**
- Reflection: "As programmers, we have power to help or harm people through our code"
- Preview: "Every game you design, you'll think about: Is it fair? Does it respect players?"
- Introduce "Ethics Checklist" students will use when designing their games

#### Assessment
- Quality of case study analysis (group worksheet)
- Depth of reasoning in discussions
- Design challenge responses (fairness thinking)

#### Exit Ticket (5 minutes)
"Name one bias you might accidentally code into a game, and how you'd prevent it."

#### Homework / Out-of-Class Extension
- Read a one-page case study (provided): How a company's biased AI caused real-world harm
- Journal reflection: "If you were that company, what would you do differently?"
- Optional: Listen to a 10-minute podcast on AI ethics (curated link)

### **LESSON 1.4: Introduction to Scratch & Computational Thinking (Thursday)**

**Duration:** 100 minutes

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand computational thinking: decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, algorithm design
- Navigate the Scratch interface
- Create a simple interactive project (non-game) to practice block-based coding
- Prepare for game design in Weeks 2–7

#### Materials Required
- Student laptops/Chromebooks (1 per student)
- Smartboard/Projector
- Internet access to Scratch (https://scratch.mit.edu/)
- Printed Scratch reference guide (1 per student)
- Sample Scratch projects for exploration (pre-selected curator list)

#### Lesson Structure

**Opening (10 minutes): What is Computational Thinking?**
- Real-world analogy: "Making a sandwich is an algorithm"
  - Break it down: Get bread, get peanut butter, get knife, spread butter, add jelly, close, cut diagonal, eat
  - Key: We broke a big task into small steps a "robot" could follow
- Introduce four pillars of computational thinking (write on board):
  1. **Decomposition:** Break big problems into small, manageable pieces
  2. **Pattern Recognition:** Find repeating patterns and structures
  3. **Abstraction:** Focus on what matters, ignore unnecessary details
  4. **Algorithm Design:** Create a step-by-step plan to solve a problem
- Today: "You'll use all four as we learn Scratch!"

**Instruction & Guided Navigation (30 minutes): Welcome to Scratch**

1. **Create & Log In** (5 min)
   - Direct all students to Scratch.mit.edu
   - Guide account creation (name, email; note: 13+ policy)
   - Discuss: Scratch is a global community of young programmers
   
2. **The Scratch Interface Tour** (15 min)
   - Project on Smartboard while students follow along
   - Identify and label each section:
     - **Stage:** Where your project displays (like a movie screen)
     - **Sprite:** The character/object you control (default is Scratchy cat)
     - **Blocks Palette:** Menu of code blocks to drag and drop (color-coded: Motion, Looks, Sound, etc.)
     - **Script Area:** Where you assemble blocks (like building LEGO)
   - Navigate through block categories together
   - Key blocks preview: Motion (move, turn), Looks (say, change color), Events (when clicked), Control (if/then, repeat)

3. **First Code: "Make Scratchy Move"** (10 min)
   - Class codes together on the board/projection
   - Drag blocks: Event ("When green flag clicked") → Motion ("move 10 steps") → Looks ("say Hello!")
   - Click green flag to run; celebrate success!
   - Discuss: "Each block is a tiny instruction. Together they make an algorithm"

**Hands-On Practice (45 minutes): "Create Your Interactive Character"**

**Part 1: Follow-Along Build (20 minutes)**
1. Each student creates a new Scratch project titled "My Character"
2. Teacher demonstrates; students replicate each step on their own laptops:
   - Change the sprite to a character they like (library > costumes)
   - Add a "When green flag clicked" event
   - Add "move 10 steps" and "turn 15 degrees" motion blocks (repeat)
   - Add a "When key pressed" event to move with arrow keys
   - Add "say" blocks with dialogue when clicked
3. Test and celebrate individual projects

**Part 2: Creative Challenge (20 minutes)**
1. Challenge: "Teach your character to do something"
   - Make it change color and size
   - Add sound effects
   - Create multiple costumes and animate them
   - Make it respond to different keys
2. Encourage experimentation; circulate to provide support
3. Optional: Explore sample Scratch projects in library for inspiration

**Part 3: Peer Sharing (5 minutes)**
- Volunteers demo their character (screen share or move around room)
- Celebrate creativity and problem-solving

**Closing & Connection (10 minutes): From Scratch to Games to AI**
- Debrief: "You just used all four pillars of computational thinking!"
  - Decomposition: Broke animation into steps
  - Pattern: Noticed "repeat" when same action happens multiple times
  - Abstraction: Didn't worry about how Scratch works internally; just used blocks
  - Algorithm: Sequenced blocks to make a plan
- Bridge to next weeks: "This same Scratch will power the games you design. And those games will contain AI!"
- Preview: "In Week 2, you'll make a full game. In Weeks 3–7, you'll add AI that learns and adapts"
- Question: "What game would you like to build? How might AI make it better?"

#### Assessment
- Navigation of Scratch interface (observation)
- Successful completion of "move character" exercise
- Creativity and experimentation in challenge phase
- Understanding of computational thinking concepts

#### Exit Ticket (5 minutes)
"Describe one algorithm you created in Scratch today using four steps. Then name a real-world algorithm (like making breakfast)."

#### Homework / Out-of-Class Extension
- Explore 3 Scratch projects from the Featured or Trending sections
- Spend 15 minutes adding features to their character project
- Sketch a game idea on paper (character, setting, one game mechanic)—bring to Week 2

# WEEK 2: Game Design Fundamentals & Scratch Coding Basics

## Overview
Week 2 transitions from AI foundations to practical game design and coding. Students design their first game, begin Scratch implementation, and think about how AI could enhance gameplay.

---

### **LESSON 2.1: Game Design Process & Mechanics (Monday)**

**Duration:** 100 minutes

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand the iterative game design process: ideation → prototyping → testing → refinement
- Define and recognize game mechanics (rules and interactive systems)
- Sketch a game concept with clear mechanics
- Identify how AI could enhance a game mechanic

#### Materials Required
- Smartboard/Projector
- Student laptops/Chromebooks
- Large paper for sketching (A3 or poster-sized)
- Colored markers, pencils
- Printed "Game Mechanics" reference sheet
- Index cards for brainstorm
- Video: "How Games are Designed" (3–4 min, curated)

#### Lesson Structure

**Opening (10 minutes): Analyzing Your Favorite Game**
- Pair-share: "What's your favorite game? What rule makes it fun?"
- Popcorn responses; write mechanics on board:
  - Jumping on platforms
  - Collecting coins
  - Defeating enemies
  - Solving puzzles
  - Racing to finish
- Reveal: "Each of these is a game mechanic—a rule or interactive system"

**Instruction (25 minutes): The Game Design Process**

1. **The Four Stages of Design** (15 min)
   - **Ideation:** Brainstorm ideas, ask "What if...?"
   - **Prototyping:** Build a rough, playable version quickly
   - **Testing:** Have others play and give feedback
   - **Iteration:** Refine based on feedback, repeat process
   - Emphasize: This is NOT linear; you might cycle through multiple times
   - Show on board as a cycle (not arrow)
   
2. **Game Mechanics: The Heart of Gameplay** (10 min)
   - Definition: Rules and interactive systems that define how a game works
   - Examples:
     - Platformer: "Jump" mechanic (press space to go up, gravity pulls down)
     - Puzzle: "Rotate blocks" mechanic (click to spin, fit into gaps)
     - RPG: "Collect items" and "gain experience" mechanics
     - Racing: "Accelerate and brake" mechanics
   - Key insight: Mechanics create challenge and fun
   - Hook AI moment: "In Weeks 3–7, your NPC AI will respond to and learn these mechanics"

**Video & Discussion (15 minutes): Real Design Stories**
- Watch: "How Games are Designed" (curated 3–4 min video showing designer discussing their game)
- Pause to highlight:
  - "They playtested with real players and changed the game"
  - "They found this mechanic didn't work and removed it"
  - "They noticed players wanted this feature and added it"
- Discussion: "How is this like scientific experimentation?" (hypothesis → test → analyze → iterate)

**Guided Practice (40 minutes): "Design Your Game" Workshop**

**Step 1: Brainstorm Your Game Concept (15 minutes)**
1. Each student gets index cards
2. Brainstorm prompts (choose one or create their own):
   - "Design a game where the player is a _____ (animal, robot, explorer, etc.)"
   - "Design a game that teaches a skill (math, spelling, etc.)"
   - "Design a game based on your favorite movie/book/show"
3. On cards, answer:
   - What is the core goal of your game? (What does the player do to win/succeed?)
   - What are the main mechanics? (Write 2–3 rules)
   - Who is your target player? (Age? Experience level?)
4. Circulate; ask clarifying questions to deepen thinking

**Step 2: Sketch Your Game on Large Paper (20 minutes)**
1. Distribute large paper and markers
2. Each student creates a visual sketch of:
   - The game's main scene/screen
   - Key visual elements (player character, enemies, obstacles, collectibles)
   - Simple annotations labeling mechanics (e.g., "jump here," "collect coins," "avoid spike")
3. Encourage: "It doesn't have to be perfect art; it's a blueprint!"
4. Post sketches around the room

**Step 3: Gallery Walk & Peer Feedback (5 minutes)**
- Students walk around, leave sticky-note feedback on others' designs:
  - "This mechanic is cool because..."
  - "I'd like to know more about..."
  - "The art style is..."

**Closing (10 minutes): Bridging to AI & Next Steps**
- Discuss: "How could AI make your game better?"
  - AI NPC that learns player patterns
  - AI difficulty adjustment
  - AI that generates content (new levels, challenges)
  - AI that personalizes the game experience
- Preview: "Starting Week 3, you'll code your game in Scratch and add AI!"
- Homework clarification: Refine game concept based on feedback

#### Assessment
- Clarity of game concept (goals, mechanics, target player)
- Quality of sketch (clear visual representation)
- Depth of feedback given to peers

#### Exit Ticket (5 minutes)
"Name your game's core mechanic and describe how AI could make it more fun or challenging."

#### Homework / Out-of-Class Extension
- Refine game concept sketch based on peer feedback
- Write a one-paragraph game description: "My game is about... Players will... The main mechanic is..."
- Play a game online and identify 3 mechanics used (bring list to next class)

### **LESSON 2.2: Sprites, Events & Movement in Scratch (Tuesday)**

**Duration:** 100 minutes

#### Learning Objectives
- Create and customize sprites in Scratch
- Implement event-triggered actions (click, key press, message)
- Code movement and animation
- Build the foundation for a playable game prototype

#### Materials Required
- Student laptops/Chromebooks (1 per student)
- Smartboard/Projector
- Scratch project template (optional, provided)
- Printable "Sprite and Event" reference guide
- Scratch block library cheat sheet

#### Lesson Structure

**Opening (10 minutes): Recap & Goal**
- Recap Week 1: Scratch interface, blocks, computational thinking
- Today's goal: "Build a playable character you can control with the keyboard"
- By end of lesson: "Your character will move, animate, and respond to events"

**Instruction & Live Coding Demo (25 minutes): Sprites & Events**

1. **Sprites: Your Game's Actors** (8 min)
   - Definition: A sprite is a character or object in your game
   - Scratch has a library of pre-made sprites; you can also upload your own
   - Each sprite can have multiple costumes (like animation frames)
   - Live demo: Change sprite in editor, show library, select a character
   - Discuss: "Every sprite can move, look, sound, and think"

2. **Events: Triggering Actions** (8 min)
   - Definition: An event is something that happens (click, key press, etc.)
   - Events trigger code blocks to run
   - Common events in games:
     - "When green flag clicked" (start the game)
     - "When key pressed" (player controls)
     - "When sprite clicked" (interactive objects)
     - "When I receive message" (one sprite tells another to do something)
   - Live demo: Create a simple event block, show it running code

3. **Movement & Control** (9 min)
   - Core blocks:
     - "move 10 steps" (go forward in direction sprite is facing)
     - "turn 15 degrees" (rotate clockwise)
     - "go to x: y:" (teleport to a position)
     - "change x by 10" (move horizontally)
   - Live demo: Build a simple movement algorithm:
     ```
     When green flag clicked
       Repeat until [touching edge]
         Move 5 steps
     ```
   - Show sprite walking across stage
   - Discuss: "The repeat block is looping—it keeps running the same steps"

**Guided Practice (55 minutes): Build a Controllable Character**

**Part 1: Sprite Setup (10 minutes)**
- All students create a new Scratch project: "My Game V1"
- Choose a sprite from library (or upload custom art if they have it)
- Optional: Change background to a simple scene

**Part 2: Implement Keyboard Controls (25 minutes)**
- Teacher models live; students replicate on their laptops
- Build event blocks for movement:
  ```
  When [right arrow] key pressed
    Turn 15 degrees right
  ```
  ```
  When [left arrow] key pressed
    Turn 15 degrees left
  ```
  ```
  When [up arrow] key pressed
    Move 10 steps (forward)
  ```
- Students test each key; debug if needed
- Celebrate: "Your character now responds to player input!"

**Part 3: Add Animation (15 minutes)**
- Introduce costumes (animation frames)
- Steps:
  1. Sprite may already have multiple costumes (check costumes tab)
  2. Create a simple walking animation:
     ```
     When [up arrow] key pressed
       Next costume
       Move 10 steps
     ```
  3. Test; show smooth walking animation
- If time: Add a second animated action (e.g., jumping, attacking) on a different key

**Part 4: Creative Enhancement (10 minutes)**
- Challenge: "Add one interactive feature"
  - Play a sound when you press a key
  - Say something when clicked
  - Change color or size with a key press
  - Rotate in place
- Circulate; celebrate creative additions

**Closing (5 minutes): Connection to Games & AI**
- Debrief: "You just built the player control system—the heart of a game!"
- Preview: "Next, you'll add obstacles, enemies, scoring, and AI mechanics"
- Question: "How could AI predict what key you'll press next?"

#### Assessment
- Successful implementation of keyboard controls
- Character animation working smoothly
- Debugging/problem-solving approach
- Creative enhancement implemented

#### Exit Ticket (5 minutes)
"Show me your character moving with keyboard controls. Tell me one thing it does and one thing you could add next."

#### Homework / Out-of-Class Extension
- Refine character controls (adjust speed, animation smoothness)
- Design an enemy or obstacle sprite on paper; sketch 2–3 costume variations for animation
- Optional: Explore Scratch projects with complex movement (study how they coded it)

### **LESSON 3.3: Difficulty Balancing & AI Personalization (Wednesday)**

**Duration:** 100 minutes

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand the concept of adaptive difficulty
- Implement difficulty scaling based on performance metrics
- Design personalized game experiences using player data
- Apply AI ethics: fairness in personalization

#### Materials Required
- Student laptops/Chromebooks (1 per student)
- Smartboard/Projector
- Student Scratch projects
- Printable "Difficulty Scaling" guide
- Sample project showing difficulty modes
- Ethical case studies: "Personalization Gone Wrong"

#### Lesson Structure

**Opening (10 minutes): The Sweet Spot of Challenge**
- Hook: "Ever played a game that was too easy? Too hard? Just right?"
- Concept: **Flow** = when challenge matches skill (neither bored nor frustrated)
- Example: Easy → boring. Hard → frustrating. Medium (just right) → flow state!
- Game designer job: Keep player in flow by adjusting challenge
- Today: "We'll code AI that adjusts difficulty to match player skill"

**Instruction (25 minutes): Adaptive Difficulty & Personalization**

1. **What is Difficulty Scaling?** (8 min)
   - Definition: Automatically adjusting game challenge based on player performance
   - Traditional: Easy, Medium, Hard modes (player picks at start)
   - Adaptive: Game watches player and adjusts in real-time
   - Benefits: Everyone can enjoy the game (skilled and novice players alike)
   - Methods:
     - Change enemy speed/health
     - Increase/decrease number of enemies
     - Change puzzle complexity
     - Adjust resource availability
   - Example: "Player winning 5 in a row? Add second enemy. Losing 3 times? Reduce enemy speed"

2. **Metrics for Measuring Performance** (10 min)
   - What does the AI track?
     - Win/loss ratio (winning % of attempts)
     - Speed of completion (fast vs. slow)
     - Score (high vs. low)
     - Deaths/mistakes (few vs. many)
   - Variables to create:
     - `wins` and `losses` (calculate win ratio)
     - `consecutive_wins` and `consecutive_losses`
     - `average_time_per_level`
   - Pseudocode:
     ```
     If win_ratio > 70%
       Difficulty = too easy, increase
     Else if win_ratio < 40%
       Difficulty = too hard, decrease
     Else
       Difficulty = just right
     ```

3. **Personalization: Tailoring Experience** (7 min)
   - Beyond difficulty: Personalize the game itself
   - Examples:
     - Player prefers platforming → fewer puzzles
     - Player prefers puzzles → fewer combat challenges
     - Player likes speed → timed challenges
     - Player likes exploration → open, non-linear levels
   - Ethical consideration: Personalization can be powerful for good or harm
   - Risk: Personalization algorithm could exploit addiction or vulnerabilities
   - Responsibility: Designers must think about unintended consequences
   - Best practice: Transparency (tell player they're being personalized) + control (let them opt out)

**Case Study Discussion (10 minutes): Personalization Ethics**
- Present scenario: "A game learns a player is most engaged by getting rare rewards. AI starts scheduling rewards to maximize engagement (even if not skill-appropriate)."
  - Question: Is this fair? Is this ethical?
  - Deeper question: "Could this exploit players, especially kids?"
- Another scenario: "An AI adjusts difficulty for each player perfectly. Everyone feels great playing."
  - Question: Is this always good?
  - Discussion: "Yes, BUT only if players have chosen to be personalized"
- Takeaway: Personalization is powerful; use responsibly

**Guided Practice (45 minutes): Implement Difficulty Scaling**

**Part 1: Design Difficulty Tiers (10 minutes)**
1. Each student designs 3 difficulty levels for their game:
   - **Easy:** What does AI do? (move slowly, telegraphs attacks, fewer enemies)
   - **Medium:** What does AI do? (normal speed, moderate challenges)
   - **Hard:** What does AI do? (fast, unpredictable, many enemies)
2. Document on template: "Difficulty Tiers"
   - Include specific variable changes (speed: 3 → 5 → 7, enemy count: 1 → 2 → 3)

**Part 2: Implement Adaptive Difficulty in Scratch (25 minutes)**
1. Create difficulty variable:
   ```
   Create [difficulty] = "medium"
   ```
2. Track performance:
   ```
   When game ends with win
     Change [wins] by 1
   When game ends with loss
     Change [losses] by 1
   ```
3. Calculate performance ratio and adjust:
   ```
   Every second
     If ([wins] / ([wins] + [losses])) > 0.7
       Set [difficulty] to "hard"
     Else if ([wins] / ([wins] + [losses])) < 0.4
       Set [difficulty] to "easy"
     Else
       Set [difficulty] to "medium"
   ```
4. Apply difficulty to NPC:
   ```
   If [difficulty] = "easy"
     Set [enemy_speed] to 3
   Else if [difficulty] = "hard"
     Set [enemy_speed] to 7
   ```
5. Test: Play multiple rounds; watch difficulty adjust

**Part 3: Personalization Feature (Optional, 10 minutes)**
- If time: Add a personalization preference
  - Example: Player chooses "I like combat" or "I like puzzles"
  - AI adjusts challenges accordingly
  - Include UI element (button or setting) so player can change preference
  - Ethical note: "Player controls personalization, not AI alone"

**Closing (10 minutes): Fairness in Design**
- Debrief: "You balanced challenge with AI learning"
- Ethical lesson: "You made a decision about using player data responsibly"
- Question: "Would you play a game that adjusts itself for you? Why or why not?"
- Preview: Next lesson—combining these AI techniques

#### Assessment
- Quality of difficulty tier design (clear, specific parameters)

- Write a one-paragraph analysis: "Is their personalization ethical?"

**Instruction (25 minutes): Procedural Generation Concepts**

1. **What is Procedural Generation?** (10 min)
   - Definition: Using algorithms to automatically create game content

3. **AI for Narrative: LLMs** (5 min)

   - Ethical note: AI-generated content is good tool, not replacing human creativity

### **LESSON 4.2: Sophisticated NPC Interactions & State Machines (Tuesday)**

**Duration:** 100 minutes

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand state machines (NPC behavior modes and transitions)
- Implement complex NPC logic with multiple behavior states
- Design NPC interactions with personality and responsiveness
- Recognize state machines in real systems

#### Materials Required
- Student laptops/Chromebooks (1 per student)
- Smartboard/Projector
- Scratch projects
- Printable "State Machine" guide and flowchart template
- Sample Scratch NPC project with states
- Videos: "Character Design & Behavior" (2–3 min)

#### Lesson Structure

**Opening (10 minutes): NPCs with Personality**
- Hook: Show 30-second clip of game NPC with complex behavior
  - Patrols normally
  - Becomes alert when player is near
  - Attacks aggressively if player gets too close
  - Retreats if outnumbered
  - Returns to patrol when player leaves
- Question: "How does the NPC 'decide' what to do?"
- Answer: State machine—NPC has different behavior modes that transition based on conditions

**Instruction (25 minutes): State Machines & NPC Behavior**

1. **What is a State Machine?** (10 min)
   - Definition: A system with distinct states (modes) and rules for transitions
   - States: Patrol, Alert, Attack, Retreat, Rest, Celebrate, etc.
   - Transitions: Conditions that cause state change
   - Example state machine for enemy:
     ```
     [Patrol] ← sees player → [Alert]
     [Alert] ← player gets close → [Attack]
     [Attack] ← takes damage → [Retreat]
     [Retreat] ← player leaves → [Patrol]
     ```
   - Benefit: Feels intelligent and responsive; complex behavior from simple rules
   - Real-world examples: Traffic light (states: red, yellow, green), thermostat, game menus

2. **Implementing State Machines in Scratch** (10 min)
   - Method: Create a variable `state` or `mode`
   - Set state values: "patrol", "alert", "attack", "retreat"
   - Code transitions:
     ```
     If [state] = "patrol"
       Do patrol behavior
       If [distance to player] < 150
         Set [state] to "alert"
     ```
   - For each state, have its own behavior loop
   - Example:
     ```
     If [state] = "alert"
       Move toward player
       If [distance to player] < 50
         Set [state] to "attack"
       Else if [player_lost]
         Set [state] to "patrol"
     ```
   - Key: Each state has its own logic AND its own transition rules

3. **Adding Personality** (5 min)
   - States can have emotional/personality aspects
   - Example: Angry enemy vs. scared enemy
     - Angry: Attacks aggressively, chases far
     - Scared: Retreats, hides, trembles (animation)
   - Use costumes/animations for visual personality
   - Use sounds for emotional tone (snarling vs. whimpering)
   - Code: `If [personality] = "angry"...` within state logic

**Video & Discussion (5 minutes):**
- Show clip of NPC with personality (e.g., from a popular game)
- Discuss: "What states does this NPC have? How does its personality come through?"

**Guided Practice (50 minutes): Design & Implement State Machine NPC**

**Part 1: Plan State Machine (15 minutes)**
1. Each student designs an NPC state machine:
   - How many states? (minimum 3: patrol, alert, attack; or patrol, alert, retreat)
   - What transitions between states? (conditions)
   - What happens in each state? (behavior, animation, sound)
   - What's the personality? (Aggressive? Curious? Cowardly? Friendly?)
2. Draw flowchart/diagram:
   ```
   [State 1] ← condition → [State 2]
   [State 2] ← condition → [State 3]
   etc.
   ```
3. Example diagram provided

**Part 2: Implement in Scratch (30 minutes)**
1. Create `state` variable: set to initial state (e.g., "patrol")
2. For each state, code behavior and transitions:
   ```
   Forever
     If [state] = "patrol"
       Move 3 steps
       If touching edge
         Turn 180 degrees
       If [distance to player] < 150
         Set [state] to "alert"
         Change [emotion] to "alert"
   
     If [state] = "alert"
       Point towards player
       Move 5 steps
       Change [color] effect to 25 (visual change)
       If [distance to player] < 50
         Set [state] to "attack"
       Else if [distance to player] > 200
         Set [state] to "patrol"
   
     If [state] = "attack"
       Move 7 steps
       Point towards player
       Play sound "roar"
       If [touching player]
         Do attack action
       If [enemy_health] < 10
         Set [state] to "retreat"
   
     If [state] = "retreat"
       Move away from player
       If [distance to player] > 300
         Set [state] to "patrol"
   ```
3. Test each state carefully
4. Debug transitions: Use "say" blocks to verify state changes

**Part 3: Add Personality Features (5 minutes)**
- Add costumes/animation that changes with state
- Add sounds
- Change colors or size based on emotional state
- Example: Alert NPC flashes red; Retreating NPC shrinks

**Closing (10 minutes): From State Machines to Real AI**
- Debrief: "You implemented a sophisticated behavior system"
- Connection: Real AI systems (chatbots, recommendation engines) use similar state transitions
- Reflection: "Your NPC feels intelligent because it responds contextually"
- Preview: "Week 5: Combining all AI systems into a polished, publishable game"

#### Assessment
- Quality of state machine design (clear states and transitions)
- Correct implementation in Scratch (each state has behavior and transitions)
- Personality visible in design (costumes, sounds, colors)
- Smooth state transitions (no bugs, logical)

#### Exit Ticket (5 minutes)
"Describe your NPC's three main states and the conditions that trigger transitions between them."

#### Homework / Out-of-Class Extension
- Add 4th or 5th state to NPC (e.g., "celebrate" or "confused")
- Implement personality variation (angry vs. scared version of same NPC)
- Document state machine on paper (flowchart)
- Play your game and test all state transitions thoroughly

### **LESSON 4.3: LLMs in Games & AI Ethics Deep Dive (Wednesday)**

**Duration:** 100 minutes

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand Large Language Models (LLMs) and their capabilities/limitations
- Explore LLM applications in gaming (dialogue, narrative, procedural storytelling)
- Deepen AI ethics knowledge: bias, misuse, environmental impact
- Design responsible AI systems

#### Materials Required
- Student laptops/Chromebooks (1 per student)
- Smartboard/Projector
- Access to ChatGPT, Claude, or similar LLM (teacher demo + optional student use)
- Printable "LLM Case Studies" handouts
- Ethical framework guide
- Video: "How LLMs Work" (5–7 min, age-appropriate, Khan Academy or similar)

#### Lesson Structure

**Opening (10 minutes): What are LLMs?**
- Hook: Show ChatGPT generating a paragraph of game dialogue
  - Prompt: "Write a monologue for a villain in a video game"
  - Output: Natural-sounding, dramatic dialogue
- Question: "How did it do that? Is it actually intelligent?"
- Today: Understanding LLMs and their role in gaming (and AI ethics)

**Instruction (30 minutes): LLMs, Gaming & Ethics**

1. **What is an LLM?** (8 min)
   - Definition: A Large Language Model is an AI trained on massive amounts of text
   - How it works (simplified):
     - Trained on billions of words from the internet, books, etc.
     - Learned patterns: "These words usually go together"
     - When you give it a prompt, it predicts the next word, then the next, and so on
   - Capability: Generate human-like text for any topic
   - Limitation: Can hallucinate (make up false information), reflects biases in training data
   - Not truly "understanding" like humans, but very good pattern matching

2. **LLMs in Gaming** (10 min)
   - **Dynamic Dialogue:** NPC dialogue that varies based on context
     - Instead of pre-written lines, NPC generates unique responses
     - Example: Player says "Hello," NPC generates a greeting that's appropriate to its character
   - **Narrative Generation:** Story branches that adapt to player choices
     - Each player gets a slightly different story
     - LLM writes narrative descriptions that fit the context
   - **Quest Generation:** Procedurally generated quests with coherent descriptions
     - Example: "Quest: Retrieve the [random item] from the [random location] for the [random NPC]"
   - **In-game Assistance:** AI tutors or companions that provide guidance
   - Limitations in games:
     - Slow (needs internet connection sometimes)
     - Can be inconsistent (same NPC might give different advice)
     - Requires careful prompting to keep characters consistent
   - Best practice: Use LLM as a tool, not a replacement for human writers

3. **Advanced AI Ethics: The Dark Side** (12 min)
   - Bias in AI: LLMs trained on human text inherit human biases
     - Example: LLM may generate stereotypical descriptions (gender stereotypes, racial stereotypes)
     - In games: This could perpetuate harmful representations
     - Solution: Carefully review generated content; fine-tune or use curated datasets
   
   - Misuse: LLMs can be weaponized
     - Example: AI generating targeted harassment, misinformation, spam
     - In games: Bot armies harassing players, AI generating abusive chat
     - Responsibility: Game designers must prevent misuse
   
   - Environmental Impact: Training LLMs requires enormous computing power
     - Data: Millions of kilotons of CO2 emitted to train a large LLM
     - Cost: Expensive to run; not accessible to everyone
     - Fairness: Privilege to those with resources
     - Reflection: "Is it ethical to use resource-intensive AI for games?"
   
   - Authenticity & Deception: Can LLMs undermine human creativity?
     - Concern: Game writers losing jobs to AI
     - Opportunity: AI as co-creator, not replacement (human + AI partnership)
     - Responsibility: Acknowledge AI's role (transparency)
   
   - Privacy: LLMs trained on user data
     - Many LLMs trained on internet text, including personal information
     - Ethical question: "Should AI be trained on people's personal information without consent?"

**Video (5 minutes):**
- Watch: "How Large Language Models Work" (Khan Academy or similar, 5–7 min)
- Highlight: Pattern matching, not true understanding

**Case Studies & Discussion (20 minutes):**

1. **Case A: Stereotypical NPC Dialogue**
   - A game developer uses an LLM to generate NPC dialogue
   - The LLM produces stereotypical descriptions ("The barbarian is strong but dumb," "The elf is graceful but weak")
   - Players complain: "This is offensive and perpetuates stereotypes"
   - Discussion: What went wrong? How do you fix it?
   - Answer: Developers need to review generated content and adjust prompts to prevent stereotypes

2. **Case B: LLM Environmental Cost**
   - A small indie game studio considers using an LLM for dynamic dialogue
   - They learn: Each query to the LLM requires significant computing power
   - Decision: Is the improved game worth the environmental cost?
   - Discussion: How do you balance innovation with environmental responsibility?
   - Answer: Consider hybrid approaches (pre-generate dialogue offline, use LLM sparingly)

3. **Case C: AI Job Displacement**
   - Game writers worry: "Will AI replace our jobs?"
   - Some companies experiment with AI-written narratives
   - Discussion: Is this ethical? What's the responsibility to human creators?
   - Answer: AI should enhance, not replace; human oversight is essential

4. **Case D: Transparent AI**
   - A game tells players: "This NPC's dialogue was generated by an AI"
   - Players respond: "Cool! I appreciate knowing that"
   - Alternative: A game hides the fact that an AI wrote NPC dialogue
   - Discussion: Is transparency always necessary? When is it important?
   - Answer: Transparency builds trust; deception breaks it

**Guided Discussion (10 minutes): Ethical Framework**
- Present framework: When designing AI systems, ask:
  1. **Who benefits?** (Players, developers, company)
  2. **Who could be harmed?** (Minorities, workers, environment)
  3. **How transparent is it?** (Do users know AI is involved?)
  4. **Can it be misused?** (What safeguards are needed?)
  5. **Is it sustainable?** (Environmentally, socially, economically)
- Example: Using LLM for NPC dialogue
  - Benefits: More dynamic dialogue, less work for writers
  - Harms: Bias in generated content, environmental cost, job displacement
  - Transparency: Should tell players
  - Misuse: Could generate harassment; need filters
  - Sustainability: Use sparingly, hybrid approach

**Closing (5 minutes): Moving Forward Responsibly**
- Recap: "LLMs are powerful but come with responsibility"
- Your role: "As developers, you'll decide how to use AI ethically"
- Challenge: "Think critically about AI, not just 'is it cool?' but 'is it right?'"
- Preview: "Week 5: Polish your game with responsible AI integration"

#### Assessment
- Understanding of LLM capabilities and limitations
- Depth of ethical thinking (recognizing multiple stakeholders)
- Ability to analyze case studies and propose solutions
- Commitment to responsible AI design

#### Exit Ticket (5 minutes)
"Would you use an LLM for dialogue in your game? Why or why not? How would you do it ethically?"

#### Homework / Out-of-Class Extension
- Generate dialogue with ChatGPT/Claude for a game character
- Analyze: Does it have bias? Is it authentic? Would you use it?
- Write a one-page reflection: "Ethical considerations for LLMs in games"
- Research: Find a published game using AI features; evaluate its ethics
- Optional: Design an "ethical AI charter" for your game (what AI will/won't do)

### **LESSON 5.2: Audio Design & User Interface (Tuesday)**

**Duration:** 100 minutes

#### Learning Objectives

#### Materials Required

- Printable "UI/UX Best Practices" guide

- Audio accessibility guide

#### Lesson Structure

**Opening (10 minutes): Sound & User Experience**
- Comparison: Play game with sound, then without
- Difference: Sound makes gameplay satisfying, emotional, immersive
- UI question: How does a player know what to do? (Instructions, menus, feedback)
- Goal: "Create professional sound design and clear UI"

**Instruction (25 minutes): Audio & UI Design**

1. **Sound Design** (10 min)
   - **SFX (Sound Effects):** Feedback for actions
     - Collect item: Bright, positive sound (ding, chime)
     - Take damage: Harsh, negative sound (buzz, hit)
     - Win: Celebration sound (fanfare, applause)
     - Move: Footsteps (subtle, optional)
   - **Music/Ambiance:** Sets mood
     - Exploration: Calm, curious
     - Action: Fast, energetic
     - Win: Triumphant
   - Best practice: Consistent volume levels (not jarring)
   - Accessibility: Always have visual feedback too (don't rely only on sound)
   - Choices: Use free library or create custom (with Scratch sound recorder)

2. **User Interface (UI)** (10 min)
   - Definition: All visual elements player interacts with (buttons, text, icons)
   - Essential UI elements:
     - **Start Screen:** Game title, "Play" button, instructions link
     - **HUD (Heads-Up Display):** Score, lives, health (on-screen during play)
     - **Instructions Screen:** How to play, controls, objective
     - **Game Over Screen:** "You won!" or "You lost," restart button
     - **Settings Menu:** Sound on/off, difficulty selector, quit
   - Design principles:
     - **Clarity:** Text readable, buttons obvious
     - **Consistency:** Same style throughout
     - **Feedback:** Button highlights when hovered, sounds when clicked
     - **Accessibility:** Large text, high contrast, no critical info in color alone

3. **User Experience (UX)** (5 min)
   - Definition: Overall experience of using the game
   - Best practices:
     - Clear goal: "What's the objective?" (win condition obvious)
     - Smooth progression: Level difficulty increases gradually
     - Responsive controls: Player input felt immediately
     - No confusion: Players understand what's happening
     - Satisfying: Actions feel good (sound, visual feedback)
   - Accessibility considerations:
     - Closed captions for dialogue
     - Colorblind-friendly palette (avoid red/green only)
     - Keyboard-only control (don't require mouse)
     - Adjustable text size
     - Optional animations (for motion-sensitive players)

**Video & Demonstration (5 minutes):**
- Show professional game UI and explain design choices

**Guided Practice (50 minutes): Audio & UI Implementation**

**Part 1: Plan Audio & UI** (10 minutes)

   - [ ] Large enough text?
   - [ ] Sound + visual feedback?
   - [ ] Keyboard controls available?

**Part 2: Implement Audio** (15 minutes)

2. Add music (optional, if time):
   - Select background music (calm, looped, appropriate)
   - Start on game begin:
     ```

     ```
3. Test volume levels (not too loud, not too soft)

**Part 2: Implement UI Screens** (20 minutes)
1. Create start screen:
   - Broadcast "game_start" event and listen for it

     ```
       Show [title_sprite]

     When [play_button] clicked
       Hide [title_sprite]
       Hide [play_button]
     ```

2. Create HUD (score, lives):
   - Display variables on screen:
     ```
     When green flag clicked
       Show variable [score]
       Show variable [lives]
     ```
3. Create game over screen:
   - Detect win/lose condition, display appropriate screen
   - Offer restart button

**Part 4: Accessibility Check (5 minutes)**
1. Playtest with accessibility in mind:
   - Test without sound (is gameplay clear?)

### **LESSON 7.1: Advanced Machine Learning & Neural Networks (Monday)**

**Duration:** 100 minutes

#### Learning Objectives

- Recognize where deep learning is used in games
- Explore limitations and real-world challenges of ML
- Think critically about AI's future in gaming and society

#### Materials Required

- Smartboard/Projector
- "Neural Networks Explained" video (2–3 min, Khan Academy)
- Interactive demo: TensorFlow Playground or similar

- Discussion prompts

#### Lesson Structure

**Opening (10 minutes): Beyond Conditionals**
- Recap: You've coded AI using if/then logic and variables
- Question: "How do commercial games have even more sophisticated AI?"
- Answer: Neural networks and deep learning
- Hook: "Ever wonder how AlphaGo beat the world chess champion? How AI can recognize faces? Neural networks"
- Today: "Understanding the next frontier of AI"

**Instruction (25 minutes): Neural Networks Simplified**

1. **What are Neural Networks?** (12 min)
   - Definition: A computational model inspired by biological brains
   - Structure: Layers of nodes (artificial neurons)
   - How it works (simplified):
     - Input layer: Data comes in
     - Hidden layers: Process and transform data
     - Output layer: Prediction or decision
   - Learning: Network adjusts connections based on feedback
   - Training: Showed many examples until accurate
   - Analogy: Like a student learning; more practice (examples) = better accuracy
   
2. **Applications in Games** (8 min)
   - **NPC Behavior:** Neural network learns player patterns and predicts next move
   - **Difficulty Scaling:** Network learns if player is struggling/bored and adjusts
   - **Content Generation:** Network generates new levels, artwork, dialogue
   - **Opponent AI:** In chess, Go, or complex games, neural networks make intelligent moves
   - **Graphics Enhancement:** Upscaling graphics, real-time ray tracing
   - **Procedural Generation:** Creating complex game worlds
   - Example: "A neural network could be trained on thousands of game sessions to predict what a player wants next"

3. **Limitations & Challenges** (5 min)
   - Data hungry: Needs thousands/millions of examples to train
   - Black box: Hard to understand why it made a decision
   - Bias: Learns biases from training data
   - Computational cost: Expensive to train and run
   - Not general: A network trained on chess can't play checkers
   - Adversarial examples: Can be fooled by slight changes to input

**Video & Demo (10 minutes):**
- "Neural Networks Explained" (2–3 min)
- Interactive demo: TensorFlow Playground (draw on canvas; watch network learn in real-time)
  - Visualizes how network adjusts to fit data
  - Shows power and simplicity of ML

**Guided Practice (40 minutes): Explore & Discuss**

**Part 1: Case Studies** (20 minutes)
1. Present 3 case studies of AI in modern games:
   - **OpenAI's Dota 2 Bot:** Multi-agent neural networks playing complex team game
   - **AI Dungeon:** Procedural narrative using language models (like ChatGPT)
   - **Hitman AI:** Crowd simulation and pathfinding using ML
2. For each:
   - Discuss: How does it improve the game?
   - Analyze: What are its limitations?
   - Consider: Ethical implications?

**Part 2: Thoughtful Discussion** (15 minutes)
- Questions posed:
  - "Would you trust an AI to balance your game's difficulty perfectly?"
  - "Should players know when they're playing against AI neural networks?"
  - "Could a neural network be biased in ways harmful to players?"
  - "What jobs might change due to AI in game development?"
  - "Could neural networks replace human creativity?"
- Popcorn discussion; teacher facilitates

**Part 3: Future Brainstorm** (5 minutes)
- "If you had access to neural networks, what would you build?"
- Encourage wild ideas (AI that generates entire games, AI that plays against you, etc.)

**Closing (10 minutes): The Future of AI in Games**
- Debrief: "Neural networks are powerful but not magic"
- Ethical reminder: "As AI becomes more sophisticated, responsibility grows"
- Challenge: "Think critically about AI, not just 'is it cool?' but 'is it right?'"
- Preview: "Next, we'll synthesize everything into your capstone"

#### Assessment
- Understanding of neural networks (conceptual, not mathematical)
- Ability to identify ML applications in games
- Critical thinking about benefits and limitations
- Engagement with ethical considerations

#### Exit Ticket (5 minutes)
"Describe one way neural networks could improve game AI. What's one risk?"
