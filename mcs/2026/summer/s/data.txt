HOW TO USE THIS SCRIPT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Text in [BRACKETS] = instructor stage directions or timing cues
- "Quoted text" = suggested exact words to say aloud
- -> means a natural transition or follow-up thought
- [KEY IDEA] = deep conceptual explanation (say or adapt to the age of the room)
- [PAUSE] = student interaction moment -- stop and wait for responses
- [ROBOT] = the daily AI Moment tie-in (5-10 min, folded in, never a lecture)
- TIER A = Grades 1-3 scaffolding | TIER B = Grades 3-5 core | TIER C = Grades 6-9 stretch

PACING MAP (from the syllabus Daily Rhythm):

================================================================================
                     PART 1 -- WARM-UP (UNPLUGGED)  [~10 min]
                          No screens. Brains on.
================================================================================


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 1 -- TITLE & WELCOME
[Duration: ~2 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the title slide as students settle. Write "Sequencing & Events"
on the board. Keep laptops CLOSED -- this is a no-screen warm-up. Bright,
welcoming energy: this is only Session 3 and many of these students have
never coded before.]

"Good afternoon, coders! Welcome to Session 3. Yesterday we wrote our
very first algorithm on paper -- our morning routine. Today we bring
those steps to life on the screen for the first time, in Scratch."

"Two big words today, and by the end of class you will own both of them:
SEQUENCING and EVENTS. Say them with me. Sequencing... events. Great."

"Here is the promise for today: by the end of this class, you will have
a real character -- we call it a sprite -- that moves and reacts to you.
You press a key, and it does something. That is a real, working, interactive
program. And you built it."

[KEY IDEA -- say slowly, this frames the whole lesson]:
"Everything a computer does -- every game, every app, every AI -- comes
down to two questions. First: WHAT steps happen, and in WHAT order? That
is sequencing. Second: WHEN do those steps start? That is an event. Order
and timing. That is the whole secret. Master those two ideas and you
understand the skeleton of every program ever written."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 2 -- WARM-UP: THE DOMINO PUZZLE (UNPLUGGED)
[Duration: ~5 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[No screens. This is the "brains on" logic puzzle. If you have real
dominoes or index cards, stand a few up at the front. If not, draw them
on the board. Physical is better -- let a student push the first one.]

"Before we touch a single computer, I want your brain to think like a
computer. Look at the front. I have set up ten dominoes in a line."

"QUESTION 1: If I push the very FIRST domino -- what happens?"

[PAUSE -- let the whole class answer out loud. They will say: they all
fall! Push it and let them watch. Celebrate the chain reaction.]

"Every domino falls, one after another, in ORDER. Domino 1 knocks 2,
2 knocks 3, and so on. That is a sequence -- steps that happen one at a
time, each one depending on the one before it."

"QUESTION 2: Now watch. I am going to take domino number 5 OUT and leave
a gap. I push the first one again. What happens now?"

[PAUSE -- let them predict BEFORE you push. Then push. The chain stops
at the gap. Sit in that moment.]

"It stops! The gap breaks the chain. Dominoes 1 through 4 fall -- and
then nothing. The rest just stand there."

"QUESTION 3 -- turn and tell your neighbor: How is this like a computer
program?"

[PAUSE -- 60 seconds of neighbor talk. Then call on 2-3 pairs. Listen
for: "if you skip a step it breaks," "the order matters."]

[KEY IDEA]:
"You just discovered the single most important truth in programming. A
program is a chain of instructions. Skip one step -- leave a gap -- and
the whole thing breaks. This is why coders are so careful about ORDER.
The computer does exactly what you tell it, in exactly the order you
tell it. It cannot 'figure out' the missing step. It just stops, or does
the wrong thing. Today we learn to write instructions that work every
single time."

[TIER A note: keep it purely physical and celebratory -- the dominoes
ARE the lesson. TIER C: ask them the sharper version -- "A real program
has thousands of steps. How would you FIND the missing domino -- the
gap -- in a chain that long? That searching-for-the-gap is called
debugging, and we do it every single day in this class."]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 3 -- CORE CONCEPT: WHAT IS A SEQUENCE?
[Duration: ~3 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the definition slide with the Morning Routine example --
"In Real Life" next to "In Scratch." Still no student screens.]

"Let's lock in the definition."

"A SEQUENCE is a list of steps carried out in order, one at a time. Miss
a step, or change the order, and the result changes."

"Look at the morning routine on the slide. In real life: wake up, brush
teeth, eat breakfast, go to school. What happens if I swap two steps --
if I 'go to school' BEFORE I 'get dressed'?"

[PAUSE -- let them laugh. "You'd be in pajamas at school!" Exactly.]

"Order matters. In real life AND in code. On the right side of the slide
you can see the same idea as Scratch blocks, stacked top to bottom. The
computer reads them the same way you read a recipe -- from the top, one
line at a time. Skip step 3 of a recipe and dinner burns. Skip step 3
of your code and your program breaks."

[KEY IDEA]:
"Here is a rule you will use for the rest of your life as a coder: the
computer reads from top to bottom, one block at a time, and never skips
ahead. It has no common sense. It will not 'know what you meant.' It only
knows the order you gave it. That is both the challenge and the beauty
of coding -- you are in complete control of the order."


================================================================================
              PART 2 -- MINI-LESSON (ONE CONCEPT, LIVE)  [~15-20 min]
                    Teacher demos on the big screen only.
================================================================================


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 4 -- CORE CONCEPT: WHAT IS AN EVENT?
[Duration: ~4 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Now open Scratch on the projector -- YOUR screen only. Students still
watch, hands off keyboards. Show the Scratch editor with the cat sprite.]

"We know a sequence is a list of steps in order. But here is the missing
piece: WHEN does the sequence start? What makes it GO?"

"That is an EVENT. An event is a TRIGGER -- something that happens which
tells your program to START running a sequence. The sentence to remember
is: 'When _____ happens, do _____.'"

[Point to the green flag at the top of the Scratch stage.]

"In Scratch, the most famous event is the GREEN FLAG. When you click the
green flag, any script that starts with the 'when green flag clicked'
block wakes up and runs its sequence. Watch."

[Demo: drag a 'when green flag clicked' hat block, snap a 'move 100
steps' under it. Click the green flag. The cat moves. Let them react.]

"See that block with the rounded top? We call it a HAT block -- it sits
on top like a hat. Every sequence needs a hat block, because the hat
block is the event that says WHEN to start."

[KEY IDEA -- the big one for today]:
"Here is the critical question on the slide: without an event, does the
code EVER run? What happens to a sprite with no green flag block on top
of its code?"

[PAUSE -- let them guess. Then demo it: pull the hat block off, leaving
a floating stack. Click the green flag. NOTHING happens.]

"Nothing! The blocks just sit there forever. Code with no event is like
a domino chain that nobody ever pushes. All the steps are ready -- but
without the trigger, they never start. The event is the finger that
pushes the first domino."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 5 -- THE 3 KEY SCRATCH EVENTS
[Duration: ~4 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the "3 Key Scratch Events" slide. Show each event block in the
Scratch palette as you name it. Demo each one live.]

"There are many events in Scratch, but today we focus on three. These
three will power almost everything you build."

"EVENT 1 -- WHEN GREEN FLAG CLICKED. The 'start the whole program' event.
This is your ON switch. Most programs begin here."

"EVENT 2 -- WHEN [SPACE] KEY PRESSED. The keyboard event. When the user
presses a key, this script fires. This is how you make things interactive
-- how the program REACTS to a person."

"EVENT 3 -- WHEN THIS SPRITE CLICKED. The mouse event. Click the character
itself, and it does something. Great for buttons and surprises."

[Demo all three live: green flag makes the cat move; space key makes it
say something; clicking the cat makes it change color or play a sound.]

[KEY IDEA]:
"Notice the pattern in all three: 'When SOMETHING happens, do SOMETHING.'
The 'something that happens' is the event. The 'do something' is the
sequence. Event on top, sequence below. This exact pattern -- wait for
a trigger, then run steps -- is how EVERY interactive program in the
world works. Your phone is doing it right now: when you tap, do this.
When a text arrives, do that. It is all events and sequences."

[TIER C stretch: "In real software this is called event-driven
programming, and the invisible loop that sits and waits for events is
called an event loop. Every app on your phone is running one right now."]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 6 -- KEY VOCABULARY: SPRITE, SCRIPT, STAGE
[Duration: ~4 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the vocabulary slide. Point to each item ON the actual Scratch
editor as you define it -- connect the word to the thing on screen.]

"Four words you will hear me use all day. Let me show you each one right
here in Scratch."

"SPRITE -- a character or object you program. The cat is a sprite. You
can pick your own -- a dog, a dragon, a Minecraft-style block, anything.
[Point to the sprite in the bottom panel.] This little guy is our sprite."

"SCRIPT -- a stack of blocks that runs together when an event fires. One
sprite can have MANY scripts. [Point to your stacked blocks.] This whole
stack, from the hat block down, is one script."

"STAGE -- the background canvas where sprites live and act. It is 480 by
360 pixels. [Point to the big white area at top.] This is the stage --
your sprite's whole world."

"HAT BLOCK -- the rounded block on top of every script that holds the
event. No hat, no start. [Point to the 'when green flag clicked' block.]"

[PAUSE -- quick check]: "Point at your neighbor and quiz them: what is a
sprite? What is a script? 20 seconds, go."

[KEY IDEA]:
"These are not just Scratch words -- they are ideas that carry into
Python and every language after. A sprite is an OBJECT. A script is a
FUNCTION or a chunk of code. The stage is the screen or the output. When
we cross over into Python in a few weeks, you will meet these same ideas
wearing different clothes. You are learning concepts, not just Scratch."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 7 -- [ROBOT] AI MOMENT: SEQUENCE = RECIPE = TRAINING STEPS
[Duration: ~4-5 minutes -- this is the daily AI tie-in, folded in]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the "AI Training Run" slide. Deliver this with wonder, not as a
lecture. This is the 5-10 minute AI Moment the syllabus builds into every
day. Keep it concrete and age-appropriate.]

"Now here is my favorite part of today -- how does this connect to
artificial intelligence, to AI?"

"You know how a sequence is a list of steps in a certain order, like a
recipe? Here is a secret: an AI is TRAINED in a sequence of steps too,
just like our scripts run in order."

"When engineers build an AI -- say, an AI that recognizes cats in photos
-- they don't just flip a switch. They run it through a sequence:
Step 1, show it thousands of example photos. Step 2, let it guess.
Step 3, tell it which guesses were right and wrong. Step 4, let it adjust.
Then repeat. Over and over, in order. That ordered sequence of steps is
called TRAINING. Same idea as our dominoes -- one step feeds the next."

[Point to the slide question: "If an AI only trained on examples of sunny
days -- what would it predict for a rainy day?"]

[PAUSE -- let them think and answer. This is a real discussion.]

"Exactly -- it would be confused, or guess wrong, because it never saw
rain in its training sequence. This is a HUGE idea in AI: a machine only
knows what it was shown, in the order it was shown. Miss a step in the
training -- leave out rainy days -- and there is a GAP, just like our
missing domino number 5. The AI breaks right at the gap."

[KEY IDEA -- the takeaway on the slide]:
"'Sequence is the skeleton of every program -- and every AI.' Whether it
is your Scratch cat or a giant AI that recognizes faces, it all starts
with steps in the right order. You are learning the real foundation
today, not a toy version."

[This plants the seed for Session 15's Teachable Machine day, where they
will SEE an AI trained on too few examples and watch it fail. Call back
to today when you get there.]


================================================================================
                 PART 3 -- GUIDED BUILD (ALL TOGETHER)  [~20 min]
                  Everyone builds the SAME thing, step by step.
================================================================================


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 8 -- GUIDED BUILD SETUP: "BY THE END YOU WILL HAVE..."
[Duration: ~2 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Students open Scratch NOW. Walk the room -- make sure every student has
the editor open with the cat sprite before you continue. Buddy-pair the
Tier A / Grade 1-2 students. Display the goal slide.]

"Screens ON now. Everyone open Scratch with the cat. Raise your hand if
you can see the cat on your stage -- good, good... [wait for all hands].
Buddies, help your partner get there."

"Here is what every single one of us will have by the end of the next
20 minutes: a cat that MOVES when you click the green flag, and SAYS
something when you press a key. Two scripts, two events, on one sprite.
We build it together, step by step. Nobody gets left behind."

"The rule for guided build: do exactly what I do, no more, no less. We
explore on our OWN projects later. Right now, we move as one."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 9 -- GUIDED BUILD: SCRIPT A (GREEN FLAG) + SCRIPT B (SPACE KEY)
[Duration: ~14 minutes -- go slowly, narrate every drag]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the step-by-step plan slide. Build on the projector one block
at a time. After EACH block, pause and physically check the room. Speak
the block names slowly. This is the guaranteed-success moment of the day.]

"SCRIPT A -- the green flag script. Follow me."

"Step 1: Click the yellow 'Events' category. Find the 'when green flag
clicked' block -- the one with the little green flag. Drag it into the
gray coding area. [Wait.] Everybody have the hat block? Show me thumbs up."

[PAUSE -- wait for a room of thumbs. Do not move on without them.]

"Step 2: Click the blue 'Motion' category. Grab 'move 100 steps.' Snap
it UNDER the hat block until you hear the click. [Wait.] Now click the
green flag at the top of the stage. Your cat should scoot to the right!"

[PAUSE -- celebrate the first movement. Big energy. Walk and check.]

"Step 3: Let's make it talk too. Purple 'Looks' category. Grab 'say
Hello! for 2 seconds.' Snap it under the move block. Click the green flag
again. It moves, THEN it says hello -- in that order, because it's a
sequence! Top to bottom, remember?"

[Point to the "Try Changing These" card:]
"Try this: change the 100 in 'move 100 steps' to 50. Click the flag.
Slower, shorter move, right? You just edited a program. Change it back
to 100."

"Now -- SCRIPT B -- a SECOND script on the SAME cat, for a key press."

"Step 4: Back to yellow 'Events.' This time grab 'when SPACE key pressed.'
Drag it to an EMPTY part of the coding area -- do NOT attach it to Script
A. Leave a gap between them. This is a brand new, separate script."

"Step 5: Under it, snap a 'say Wheee!' block, or a Motion block like
'turn 15 degrees.' Your choice. Now press the SPACE bar on your keyboard."

[PAUSE -- let the room press space. Watch the reactions.]

[KEY IDEA -- "The Big Discovery" on the slide]:
"Look what just happened. Your cat has TWO scripts. One waits for the
green flag. One waits for the space key. They do NOT interfere with each
other -- each one waits for its OWN event and runs independently. This is
huge: a sprite can have many scripts, each triggered by a different event.
That is how real interactive programs juggle many things at once."

[BUGS ARE NORMAL card -- say this warmly, it addresses the fear of
falling behind]:
"If your sprite does NOT move -- do not panic. Bugs are normal and
expected in this class. Check the number one cause: are your blocks
CONNECTED to a hat block? A floating block, not snapped under an event,
NEVER runs. It just sits there -- like a domino with no one to push it.
Snap it on tight until you hear the click."

[TIER A: green flag script only is a full success -- moving AND saying
is plenty; the space-key script is a bonus if the buddy pair gets there.
TIER C: challenge card preview -- "Can you make TWO different keys do
two different things? Hold that thought for open build."]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 10 -- GUIDED BUILD PAYOFF: "YOUR BODY IS A SPRITE"
[Duration: ~4 minutes -- SECTION 1 may shorten or skip this]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[A quick, joyful unplugged embodiment to cement the concept before the
break. Stand the class up. You play "the computer / the key."]

"Everybody stand up. We are going to BE the program. I am the keyboard.
When I press a 'key' -- when I call it out -- you run your sequence."

[Run it like Simon Says, mapped to events:]
"When I say GREEN FLAG -- take 3 steps forward and wave. [Do it together.]
When I press the SPACE key -- jump and say 'Wheee!' [Do it.]
When I CLICK THE SPRITE -- that's a tap on your own head -- spin around."

[Call events in random order a few times. Fast, fun, 90 seconds.]

"See what you just did? You waited for an EVENT, then you ran a SEQUENCE
of actions in order. Your body is a Scratch sprite! Your brain is the
computer reading the script top to bottom."

[This is the natural bridge into the movement break -- they are already
standing.]

[SECTION 1 (75 min): keep this to ONE round (~90 sec) or fold it directly
into the brain break to protect open-build time.]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 11 -- BRAIN BREAK
[Duration: ~5 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display a calm break slide. Screens down or backs to screens. This is
an essential focus reset -- honor it fully, do not skip it.]

"Great work, coders. Screens down, stand up, shake it out. Stretch to
the sky... touch your toes... roll your shoulders. Reset your focus."

"Here is a tiny thing to turn over while we stretch, no pressure: when
we come back, you get to pick YOUR OWN sprite and build YOUR OWN project.
Start dreaming up what character you want and what it should do."

[Let them move, drink water, breathe. Bring them back gently and seated.]

             PART 5 -- OPEN BUILD + CHALLENGE CARDS  [~30-40 min]
              Apply the concept to your OWN project. Explore & stretch.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 12 -- OPEN BUILD: THE THREE TIERS
[Duration: ~2 min to launch, then students build ~28-38 min]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the three tier cards. Read the goals clearly. Then release them
to build. This is the heart of the session -- the longest block. Circulate
constantly, coaching not correcting.]

"Now it is YOUR turn. Same idea -- sequencing and events -- your own
project. Find your tier goal on the slide. Everyone succeeds at their
own level, and fast finishers grab a challenge card instead of waiting."

"TIER A -- Grades 1-3: Use the starter cat. Add the green flag event.
Make it MOVE, then make it SAY something. Buddy pairs welcome and
encouraged.
   GOAL: 2 blocks attached to the green flag -- sprite moves AND says
   something."

"TIER B -- Grades 3-5: Pick YOUR OWN sprite. Create TWO scripts -- one
for the green flag with 3 or more actions, and one for a key press. Name
your project!
   GOAL: sprite does 3 actions in sequence, PLUS responds to 1 key event."

"TIER C -- Grades 6-9: TWO sprites, each with its own scripts. Each sprite
responds to a DIFFERENT key with a DIFFERENT action. Can they interact
with each other?
   CHALLENGE CARD: make sprite 2 respond to TWO different keys with two
   different actions."

"FINISHED EARLY? Add a SOUND EFFECT on a key press, help a neighbor debug,
or reach up to the next tier's goal. Nobody sits and waits in this room."

[KEY IDEA -- say before releasing them]:
"One coaching rule for open build: PREDICT before you click. Before you
press the green flag, say out loud what you THINK will happen. Then click.
If it surprises you, you just found the most valuable thing in the room --
a gap in your understanding. That surprise is where the learning is."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 13 -- OPEN BUILD COACHING NOTES (for the instructor)
[Use while circulating -- not projected]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Move constantly. Kneel to eye level. Praise effort and process out loud;
give any correction quietly and privately -- this cohort is sensitive to
public criticism.]

COMMON ISSUES & QUICK FIXES:
  - Sprite won't move -> "Is the block snapped to a HAT block? Floating
    blocks never run." (The #1 bug -- point at the gap, don't fix it for
    them; let them snap it and feel the win.)
  - Sprite moves off-screen and vanishes -> "Drag it back to center, or
    add 'go to x:0 y:0' at the top of your green-flag script to reset."
  - Nothing happens on key press -> "Check the key in the block matches
    the key you're pressing. 'space' vs 'up arrow' matters."
  - Two scripts accidentally snapped together -> "Pull them apart -- they
    need to be SEPARATE, each with its own hat block."
  - "I'm done!" (too fast) -> hand them the challenge card or the next
    tier goal; never let them idle.

WATCH FOR (celebrate publicly):
  - A student who discovers multiple scripts run independently.
  - A student debugging their own floating-block bug -- name it: "You just
    DEBUGGED! That's what real coders do all day."
  - Kind buddy behavior -- praise it loudly; it sets the culture.

TIER A support: keep them on the cat; success = move + say. Buddy them.
TIER C stretch: two-sprite interaction; ask "what event could make sprite
1 react to sprite 2?" (seeds broadcasting/messages -- a future concept).


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 14 -- 2-MINUTE WARNING & SAVE
[Duration: woven into the end of open build]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

"Two minutes, coders! Bring your project to a good stopping point. And
IMPORTANT -- SAVE your work. File menu, or take a screenshot, or copy
your project link. We do not lose the thing we just built."

[Make sure every student has saved before share-out. Walk and confirm,
especially with the youngest students.]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 15 -- SHARE-OUT + QUICK CHECK QUESTIONS
[Duration: ~7 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the share-out slide. Default to "show your neighbor." Then invite
1-2 VOLUNTEERS only. Presenting is opt-in -- we cheer for everyone.]

"Turn to your neighbor and show them your project. Then use this exact
sentence to give feedback: 'I noticed your sprite did ______. Could you
add ______ next?' Kind and specific. 90 seconds -- go."

[PAUSE -- neighbor shares. Then:]

"Would 1 or 2 volunteers like to show the whole class? Totally optional --
and we cheer for everyone who does."

[If volunteers: warm applause every time. If none: that's completely fine,
move on with zero pressure.]

[Quick concept check to lock in learning -- from the slide questions.
Fast, whole-class, hands up:]

"QUESTION 1: You have 3 blocks attached to 'when green flag clicked' --
move, say, turn. When you click the flag, the sprite will...?"
[Answer: do all three, IN ORDER, top to bottom -- move, then say, then
turn. It's a sequence.]

"QUESTION 2: A block floating in the script area, not connected to any
hat block, will...?"
[Answer: NEVER run. No event, no start -- like a domino nobody pushes.]

"QUESTION 3: Where would you find the 'when green flag clicked' block?"
[Answer: the yellow EVENTS category.]

[PAUSE -- affirm correct answers warmly; gently clarify any misses. No
one is wrong, just "not yet."]

"YOU BUILT: a real Scratch sprite that MOVES and REACTS to you. That is a

[KEY IDEA -- the takeaway line from the lesson]:
"Remember our AI moment: 'Sequence is the skeleton of every program -- and

================================================================================
APPENDIX A -- COMMON PITFALLS & HOW TO HANDLE THEM
================================================================================

PITFALL: Student's blocks are all there but the sprite does nothing.
  Handle: "Ninety percent of the time, a block is floating -- not snapped
          under a hat block. Point to your event block. Now trace down --
          is every block CLICKED into the one above it? A gap anywhere
          means the chain stops there, just like our missing domino."

PITFALL: "I clicked the green flag but I don't see the cat move."
  Handle: "It probably moved off the edge of the stage already. Click the
          flag again and watch closely -- or drag the cat back to the
          middle. To fix it for good, add a 'go to x:0 y:0' block at the
          TOP of your green-flag script so it resets every time."

PITFALL: Student attaches the space-key script onto the green-flag script.
  Handle: "These are two SEPARATE scripts, each with its own hat. Pull
          them apart. One waits for the flag, one waits for the space key
          -- they live side by side, not stacked together."

PITFALL: Young student (Grade 1-2) overwhelmed by the block palette.
  Handle: Narrow the world. "Ignore every color except yellow and blue
          right now. Yellow = when to start. Blue = how to move. That's
          all we need." Buddy them and celebrate the first movement loudly.

PITFALL: Fast Tier-C student finishes in 5 minutes and is bored.
  Handle: Hand the challenge card immediately: "Two sprites, two different
          keys, two different actions -- and then, can sprite 1 REACT when
          sprite 2 touches it?" (Seeds the 'broadcast/message' event for a
          later session.) Or invite them to mentor a younger buddy.

PITFALL: Student is nervous to share their project.
  Handle: Never cold-call. Default to neighbor-sharing. "Sharing is always
          optional here -- and we cheer for everyone." Let the project
          itself be the demonstration of learning.

PITFALL: "Why does the order of my blocks even matter? It's basically the
         same stuff."
  Handle: "Let's test it. Put 'say Hello' BEFORE 'move,' then AFTER. Click
          the flag both ways. See how it talks first then moves, versus
          moves then talks? Same blocks, different order, different result.
          That's sequencing -- order is meaning."

APPENDIX B -- STRETCH QUESTIONS FOR TIER C / ADVANCED STUDENTS
================================================================================

1. EVENT LOOP: Your phone reacts instantly to every tap, all day long.
   How do you think it "waits" for those taps? (Intro idea: an invisible
   loop that constantly listens for events -- an "event loop." We'll build
   a real one in Python later.)

2. INDEPENDENCE: You have two scripts on one sprite, one for the flag and
   one for the space key. If both events fired at the EXACT same instant,
   what would happen? Try it. (Concept seed: concurrency -- doing more than
   one thing at once.)

3. STATE: Right now the cat has no memory -- every green-flag click starts
   fresh. How could you make it remember how many times it's been clicked?
   (Seeds VARIABLES -- Session 6.)

4. AI CONNECTION: We said an AI is trained in a SEQUENCE of steps. If you
   changed the ORDER of the training examples, do you think the AI would
   learn differently? (Real, open research question -- order can matter in
   training. Great intuition for Session 15.)

5. DEBUGGING STRATEGY: If a 500-block script breaks, you can't check every
   block one by one. What's a faster way to find the "missing domino"?
   (Seeds binary search / halving the problem -- connects to the Session 12
   guessing game.)


================================================================================
APPENDIX C -- QUICK REFERENCE: TODAY'S KEY IDEAS
================================================================================

SEQUENCE:  A list of steps carried out in order, one at a time.
           Change the order -> change the result. (Recipe / dominoes.)

EVENT:     A trigger that STARTS a script. "When ___ happens, do ___."
           No event -> the code never runs.

THE 3 KEY SCRATCH EVENTS:
           1. when green flag clicked   -- start the program (ON switch)
           2. when [key] pressed        -- react to the keyboard
           3. when this sprite clicked  -- react to a mouse click

HAT BLOCK: The rounded block on top of every script that holds the event.
           No hat block -> the blocks below never run.

SPRITE:    A character/object you program.
SCRIPT:    A stack of blocks that runs together when its event fires.
STAGE:     The 480x360 canvas where sprites live.

THE ONE-LINE TAKEAWAY:
           "Sequence is the skeleton of every program -- and every AI."

AI TIE-IN: An AI is TRAINED in a sequence of steps, in order -- just like
           our scripts run in order. Miss a step (or a kind of example)
           and there's a GAP, just like a missing domino: the AI breaks
           right at the gap.
