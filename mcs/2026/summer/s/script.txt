HOW TO USE THIS SCRIPT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Text in [BRACKETS] = instructor stage directions or timing cues
- "Quoted text" = suggested exact words to say aloud
- -> means a natural transition or follow-up thought
- [KEY IDEA] = deep conceptual explanation (say or adapt to the age of the room)
- [PAUSE] = student interaction moment -- stop and wait for responses
- [ROBOT] = the daily AI Moment tie-in (5-10 min, folded in, never a lecture)
- TIER A = Grades 1-3 scaffolding | TIER B = Grades 3-5 core | TIER C = Grades 6-9 stretch

================================================================================
                     PART 1 -- WARM-UP (UNPLUGGED)  [~10 min]
                          No screens. Bodies on.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 1 -- TITLE & WELCOME  ("Scratch: Loops / Why write it 10 times when once will do?")
[Duration: ~2 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the title slide as students settle. Write "LOOPS" on the board in big
letters. Keep laptops CLOSED -- this is a no-screen, body-first warm-up. High
energy: this is the concept kids fall in love with, because it feels like a
superpower.]

"Good morning, coders! Welcome to Session 4. Yesterday you built something real
-- a sprite that MOVED and REACTED when you pressed a key. You used two big
ideas: sequencing and events. Say them back to me. Sequencing... events. Yes."

"Today we add the idea that makes coders LAZY in the best possible way. It is
called a LOOP. Say it with me. Loop. One more time. Loop. Good."

"Here is the promise on the slide: 'Why write it ten times when once will do?'
By the end of today you will make the computer do something a hundred times --
without writing it a hundred times. Three little blocks will do the work of a
hundred. That is not a trick. That is real programming."

[KEY IDEA -- say slowly, this frames the whole lesson]:
"Here is the big idea. A sequence is steps in order -- you learned that
yesterday. A LOOP is when you take a chunk of that sequence and tell the
computer: 'Do this same group of steps AGAIN, a bunch of times.' Instead of
copying, you repeat. Computers are unbelievably good at doing the same thing
over and over without getting bored, without getting tired, and without making
mistakes. A loop is how we hand them that job. Master loops and you can make a
computer do a million steps with three lines of code."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 2 -- WARM-UP: JUMPING-JACK LOOP (UNPLUGGED / MOVEMENT)
[Duration: ~5 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[No screens. Everyone STANDS UP. This is the "brains on AND bodies on" warm-up
-- the syllabus lets movement and the coding lesson roll into one here. You are
the computer; the class is the sprite running your loop.]

"Everybody stand up. I am going to say a number. When I do, you do that many
jumping jacks -- out loud, count them with me. Ready? The number is... FIVE!"

[PAUSE -- do 5 jumping jacks together, counting 1-2-3-4-5 out loud. Big energy.]

"Now here is the sneaky question. When I said 'five,' did I tell you every
single jack one at a time? Did I say 'jump, land, jump, land, jump, land'?"

[PAUSE -- let them answer. No! You just said one number -- five.]

"Exactly. I said ONE instruction -- 'do five jumping jacks' -- and you knew to
repeat the SAME move five times. That one instruction did the work of five.
That is a LOOP. In code we would write it like this --"

[Write on the board:]
    repeat 5
        jumping jack

"Read it out loud with me: 'repeat five... jumping jack.' Two lines. It ran
five jumps."

"Now watch what happens WITHOUT a loop. If I could not use the word 'repeat,'
I would have to say: jumping jack, jumping jack, jumping jack, jumping jack,
jumping jack. Five separate instructions for the exact same move. And if I
wanted a hundred? I would be here all class just writing 'jumping jack.'"

[PAUSE -- turn and tell your neighbor: what is one thing you do more than once
in a row every day? 30 seconds. Listen for: brushing (back and forth), stairs,
chewing, clapping. Everything repetitive is secretly a loop.]

[TIER A note: keep it purely physical and joyful -- the jumping jacks ARE the
lesson; the word "repeat" is the whole takeaway. TIER C: sharper version --
"If your body could ONLY understand 'repeat N times,' how would you tell it to
do 5 jacks, then 3 spins? Can one 'repeat' do both? No -- you need TWO loops.
Hold that thought; that is nested loops, and it is your challenge card today."]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 3 -- CORE CONCEPT: WHY LOOPS EXIST (10 LINES vs 3 LINES)
[Duration: ~3 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the "Mini-Lesson - One Big Idea" comparison: 10 copied lines on the
left, a tiny 3-line loop on the right. Still no student screens.]

"Let's lock in WHY loops exist. Look at the two sides of the slide."

"On the LEFT: ten lines of code to say the same simple thing ten times. Long.
Boring to type. And here is the real danger -- easy to forget one, or make a
tiny typo in line seven that you will never find."

"On the RIGHT: three lines. A loop that says 'do this ten times.' And here is
the magic -- if I want a HUNDRED instead of ten, I change the number ten to a
hundred. One character. Done. The left side would need ninety more lines."

[PAUSE -- ask: "Which one would YOU rather write? Which one has fewer places
to make a mistake?" Let them answer. The loop wins on both.]

[KEY IDEA]:
"Coders have a saying: 'Don't Repeat Yourself.' If you find yourself copying
the same blocks over and over, stop -- that is the computer telling you to use
a loop. Fewer lines means fewer bugs, easier changes, and clearer thinking.
A loop does not just save typing. It makes your program smarter and safer.
That is why loops are one of the most powerful tools in ALL of programming --
not just Scratch. Every language you ever learn will have them."

              PART 2 -- MINI-LESSON (ONE CONCEPT, LIVE)  [~15-20 min]
                    Teacher demos on the big screen only.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 4 -- CORE CONCEPT: WHAT IS A LOOP? (THE CONTROL BLOCKS)
[Duration: ~4 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Now open Scratch on the projector -- YOUR screen only. Students still watch,
hands off keyboards. Show the block palette and click into the ORANGE Control
category so they see where loops live.]

"Here is our definition, straight from the slide: a LOOP is an instruction that
says, 'Do this group of steps multiple times.' That group of steps inside the
loop is a sequence -- the same idea from yesterday -- and the loop wraps around
it and repeats it."

[Point to the Control category.]

"In Scratch, loops live in the ORANGE Control blocks. Yesterday your event
blocks -- the green flag -- were YELLOW. Today's new color is ORANGE. Whenever
you want something to repeat, go to orange."

[Demo: drag a 'when green flag clicked' hat block. Then grab a 'repeat 10'
block from Control and snap it under. Notice its shape.]

"Look at the shape of this loop block. It has a MOUTH -- an open C-shape. The
blocks you put INSIDE that mouth are the ones that repeat. Whatever is hugged
by the loop's arms runs again and again. Blocks outside the mouth run only once."

[Demo: drop a 'move 10 steps' block INSIDE the mouth of the repeat 10. Click
the green flag. The cat scoots across in ten little hops.]

[KEY IDEA]:
"Notice what just happened. I wrote ONE 'move 10 steps' block -- but it ran ten
times, because the loop repeated it. One block, ten actions. The loop is like a
photocopier for behavior: you give it one instruction and it runs it as many
times as you say. This is the whole idea. Everything else today is just choosing
HOW MANY times, and WHEN to stop."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 5 -- QUICK CHECK: repeat N  vs  forever
[Duration: ~4 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the "Quick Check" slide with Scenario A and Scenario B. This is the
key decision of the day: does the loop have a fixed count, or does it run until
the program stops? Show both loop blocks -- 'repeat 10' and 'forever' -- in the
Control palette.]

"There are two kinds of loops we meet today, and telling them apart is the most
important skill of this lesson."

"LOOP 1 -- 'repeat N.' You give it a NUMBER. It runs that exact many times, then
STOPS on its own. 'repeat 4' runs four times. 'repeat 100' runs a hundred times.
It counts, then quits. Use it when you know HOW MANY."

"LOOP 2 -- 'forever.' No number. It runs and runs and NEVER stops on its own --
until you click the red stop sign or quit the program. Use it when you want
something to keep going with no set end."

[Read the slide scenarios aloud:]
"SCENARIO A -- a sprite waves hello exactly THREE times when clicked. Fixed
count, or forever?"

[PAUSE -- let them decide. Answer: repeat 3. We know the exact number: three.]

"SCENARIO B -- a bouncing ball keeps moving until the player quits the game.
Fixed count, or forever?"

[PAUSE -- let them decide. Answer: forever. There is no set number of bounces
-- it goes until the player stops.]

[KEY IDEA]:
"Here is the rule to carry forever: if you can count it, use 'repeat.' If it
should never stop on its own, use 'forever.' A firework that bursts three times
-- repeat 3. A heartbeat that never quits while you're alive -- forever. Pick
the loop that matches whether the job has an ending or not. Get this one choice
right and your program behaves exactly how you imagined."

[TIER C stretch: "There is a third kind -- 'repeat until SOMETHING is true' --
a loop that stops on a condition, not a count. We meet conditions next session.
For now, notice 'forever' is really just 'repeat until the user quits.'"]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 6 -- WATCH: VIDEO 1 -- SCRATCH REPEAT & FOREVER
[Duration: ~3 minutes video + 1 minute talk]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Play Video 1 on the projector -- a short clip showing 'repeat' and 'forever'
in action in Scratch. Keep it under 3 minutes; you can pause it on the key
moment where the loop wraps the blocks.]

"Watch closely. See how the blocks sit INSIDE the loop's mouth, and how the
'repeat' one stops but the 'forever' one just keeps cycling."

[After the video:]
"Turn to your neighbor and finish this sentence in your OWN words: 'A loop is
like...' -- 20 seconds, go."

[PAUSE -- listen for great metaphors: "like a merry-go-round," "like a song on
repeat," "like brushing back and forth." Celebrate 2-3 out loud. Their own
words mean the concept landed.]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 7 -- REAL LIFE vs CODE: SPOT THE PATTERN
[Duration: ~4 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the "Real Life vs. Code - Spot the Pattern" slide with the everyday
examples mapped to repeat counts: buttering slices (repeat 4), jumping jacks
(repeat 20), drawing a snowflake spike (repeat 6). Still teacher screen only.]

"Loops are not a computer thing -- they are an EVERYWHERE thing. Look at the
slide with me."

"BUTTERING TOAST: you butter each slice the same way. Four slices? That is
'repeat 4' -- one action, four times."

"JUMPING JACKS: up, down, up, down, for a count. Twenty of them is 'repeat 20'
-- just like our warm-up."

"DRAWING A SNOWFLAKE: draw one spike, rotate a little, and repeat. Six spikes
around a center is 'repeat 6.' The same tiny move, six times, makes a whole
snowflake."

[PAUSE -- ask: "Name ONE thing YOU do every day that is secretly a loop. Tell
your neighbor." 30 seconds. Steps to school, chewing bites, stirring, going up
stairs -- all loops.]

[KEY IDEA]:
"Once you learn loops, you start SEEING them everywhere -- in stairs, in music
choruses, in the days of the week that repeat, in your daily routine. That is
exactly how a programmer's brain grows. You look at any repeated pattern and
think, 'That's a loop -- I could tell a computer to do that in one instruction.'
You are learning to see the world the way a coder does."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 8 -- [ROBOT] AI MOMENT: "AI LEARNS BY REPEATING"
[Duration: ~4-5 minutes -- this is the daily AI tie-in, folded in]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the "AI Moment - AI learns by repeating" slide. Deliver with wonder,
not as a lecture. This is the 5-10 minute AI Moment the syllabus folds into
every day. Call back to yesterday's AI moment: sequence = training steps.]

"Now my favorite part of every day -- how does today's idea connect to AI, to
artificial intelligence?"

"Yesterday we said an AI is TRAINED in a sequence of steps. Today we add the
missing piece: that training sequence is run inside a LOOP, over and over."

"Think about an AI learning to recognize a cat in a photo. Does it look at ONE
cat picture, one time, and suddenly know cats forever?"

[PAUSE -- let them answer. No!]

"Right. It LOOPS through thousands of cat photos -- again and again -- and each
time around the loop, it gets a tiny bit better at spotting what makes a cat
look like a cat. Whiskers. Pointy ears. That certain face. Each trip through
the loop, the pattern gets a little stronger."

[Point to the slide's core line:]
"Here is the big idea on the slide: your 'repeat' block IS the training loop of
an AI. It is the exact same engine. More loops means more practice means a
smarter AI -- just like practicing piano. You don't get good at piano by playing
a song once. You LOOP it, a hundred times, and each time your fingers learn a
little more."

[PAUSE -- ask: "If you only practiced a song ONE time, would you play it well
at a concert? So why do you think an AI needs to loop thousands of times?"]

[KEY IDEA -- the takeaway]:
"'AI learns by repeating.' The 'repeat' block you are about to use to draw a
square is the same idea that powers a giant AI learning from millions of examples.
The computer isn't magic -- it's a loop that runs so many times the pattern
becomes strong. You are learning the real engine of AI today, not a toy version."

[This plants the seed for Session 15's Teachable Machine day -- where they will
WATCH a model get smarter as it loops through more examples, and fail when it
loops through too few. Call back to today when you get there.]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 8b -- WATCH: VIDEO 2 -- LOOPS IN A REAL SCRATCH GAME
[Duration: ~3 minutes video + 1 minute talk -- fold in; SECTION 1 may skip]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Play Video 2 -- a real Scratch game showing 'forever' doing the heavy lifting
(a character constantly falling, a background always scrolling, an enemy always
chasing). Keep it short.]

"Watch for the 'forever' loop. What is the ONE thing it keeps doing the whole
game?"

[After the video:]
"Finish this sentence: 'I noticed the forever loop was used to...' -- shout it
out."

[PAUSE -- listen for: "keep the ball bouncing," "make it always fall," "keep
checking for a key." Affirm: forever = the always-on engine of a game.]

[SECTION 1 (75 min): if time is tight, skip this video and simply name that
"forever" runs a game's always-on motion; go straight to the guided build.]

                 PART 3 -- GUIDED BUILD (ALL TOGETHER)  [~20 min]
                  Everyone builds the SAME thing, step by step.
================================================================================


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 9 -- GUIDED BUILD SETUP + STEP 1: DRAW A SQUARE WITH repeat 4
[Duration: ~10 minutes -- go slowly, narrate every drag]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Students open Scratch NOW. Walk the room -- confirm everyone has the editor
open with the cat before continuing. Buddy-pair the Tier A / Grade 1-2 kids.
For drawing, you need the PEN extension -- show them the "Add Extension" button
bottom-left and add "Pen" so the 'pen down' block appears. Display the goal
slide.]

"Screens ON. Everyone open Scratch with the cat. Thumbs up when you can see the
cat -- good, good... [wait for all thumbs]. Buddies, help your partner get there."

"Here is what every one of us will have by the end of the next twenty minutes:
a sprite that draws a perfect SQUARE using a loop -- and a sprite that DANCES
forever. Two loops, two payoffs. We build it together, step by step. Nobody
gets left behind. The rule for guided build: do exactly what I do, no more, no
less. Your own projects come later."

"First, one setup step. Click 'Add Extension' at the bottom-left, and choose
PEN. Now we have a 'pen down' block, so our cat leaves a line as it moves --
like a marker taped to its tail. [Wait for everyone.]"

"STEP 1 -- THE SQUARE. Let's think before we build. A square has FOUR equal
sides and FOUR turns. Each turn is ninety degrees -- a perfect corner. Four
times ninety is three hundred sixty -- one full turn all the way around. That's
WHY the shape closes into a square."

"Without a loop, I would write: move 100, turn 90, move 100, turn 90, move 100,
turn 90, move 100, turn 90. Eight blocks. But every side is the SAME -- move,
then turn. So we LOOP it."

"Step 1a: drag a 'when green flag clicked' hat block. Under it, snap 'pen down'
so the cat draws. [Wait. Thumbs up.]"

[PAUSE -- wait for a room of thumbs. Do not move on without them.]

"Step 1b: from ORANGE Control, grab 'repeat 4' and snap it under 'pen down.'
See the mouth? Whatever goes inside repeats four times. [Wait.]"

"Step 1c: INSIDE the mouth, put 'move 100 steps,' then under it 'turn 90
degrees.' Two blocks, inside the loop. That's it. Read it with me: 'repeat
four... move one hundred... turn ninety.' [Wait -- check every screen.]"

"Now -- click the green flag. Watch your cat draw a square!"

[PAUSE -- celebrate the first square. Big energy. Walk and check. Some cats
draw offscreen -- have them drag the cat to center first, or add 'go to x:0
y:0' at the top.]

[Point to the "Try all three shapes" card:]
"Here's the pattern hidden in the square. Sides times turn-angle equals 360.
Square: 4 sides, turn 90. Triangle: 3 sides, turn 120. Hexagon: 6 sides, turn
60. Change 'repeat 4' to 'repeat 3' and 'turn 90' to 'turn 120' -- you get a
triangle! The loop count and the turn angle always multiply to 360."

[KEY IDEA -- "The Big Discovery" for the square]:
"Look what you did. You wrote 'move' ONCE and 'turn' ONCE -- but the loop ran
them four times and drew a whole shape. Change one number and you get a totally
different shape. THAT is the power of a loop: a little bit of code, controlled
by one number, making something big. This is exactly how Python 'turtle'
drawing works too -- you're previewing a lesson from a few weeks out."

[TIER A: the square alone is a full success -- move + turn inside 'repeat 4.'
Buddy them and celebrate the moment the square closes. TIER C: "Try a circle --
'repeat 36,' 'move 10,' 'turn 10.' Why does that look round?"]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 10 -- GUIDED BUILD STEP 2: DANCE FOREVER (COSTUMES + forever)
[Duration: ~8 minutes -- SECTION 1 may demo this instead of all-build]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the "Guided Build - Step 2" slide. Now we meet 'forever.' The Scratch
cat has TWO costumes (costume1, costume2) -- switching them fast makes it look
like it's dancing. Build on the projector one block at a time.]

"Now the OTHER loop -- 'forever.' We're going to make the cat DANCE and never
stop. Start a brand-new script -- do NOT attach it to your square script."

"Step 2a: drag a fresh 'when green flag clicked' hat block to an EMPTY spot.
[Wait.]"

"Step 2b: from ORANGE Control, grab 'forever' -- notice it has NO number, and
its mouth has no bottom lip, because it never ends. Snap it under the hat. [Wait.]"

"Step 2c: INSIDE the forever loop, put 'next costume,' then 'wait 0.3 seconds.'
Click the flag. Your cat flips between its two costumes over and over -- it's
dancing! [Wait -- watch the room light up.]"

[PAUSE -- celebrate. Then point to the "Try changing" card:]
"Change the 0.3 to 0.1. Click the flag. The dance speeds up -- faster costume
switches. That is exactly how MOVIES work: about 24 pictures per second,
looping forward so fast your eyes see smooth motion. A loop switching pictures
IS animation."

[KEY IDEA -- the 'forever' discovery]:
"Two big things here. First: 'forever' never stops on its own -- only the red
stop sign ends it. Second: your cat now has TWO separate loop-scripts and they
do not fight each other -- one drew a square and stopped, one dances endlessly.
A sprite can run many loops, each doing its own job. That is how a real game
keeps the background scrolling AND the player jumping AND the score counting --
lots of loops, all at once."

[SECTION 1 (75 min): DEMO this on the projector and let them watch, OR let them
build just the square and preview the dance -- protect open-build time. Homework
is unchanged either way.]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 11 -- GUIDED BUILD PAYOFF: "YOUR BODY IS A LOOP"
[Duration: ~2 minutes -- SECTION 1 may fold into the brain break]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[A quick joyful embodiment before the break, echoing yesterday's "your body is
a sprite." Stand the class up. You are the computer calling the loop.]

"Everybody stand. When I say 'repeat 4 - clap,' you clap exactly four times,
counting out loud, then STOP. Ready -- repeat 4, clap! [Do it: 1-2-3-4, stop.]"

"Now: 'forever - sway' -- sway side to side and DON'T stop until I show the stop
sign. [Let them sway.] ...and STOP! [Make a stop-sign hand.]"

"See the difference in your own body? 'repeat 4' ended by itself after four.
'forever' only stopped when I made it stop. You just FELT the two loops."

[This is the natural bridge into the movement break -- they're already standing.]

SLIDE 12 -- BRAIN BREAK  ("Do 10 jumping jacks = repeat 10 on your body!")
[Duration: ~5 minutes]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the calm break slide. This is an essential focus reset -- honor it
fully, do not skip it. It also happens to be a perfect loop.]

"Screens down, stand up, shake it out. And to reset -- do TEN jumping jacks with

"Now shake out your hands, three big deep breaths... in... and out. Roll your
shoulders. Reset your focus."

"Tiny thing to dream about while we breathe, no pressure: when we come back, YOU

[Let them move, drink water, breathe. Bring them back gently and seated.]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 13 -- OPEN BUILD: THE THREE TIERS
[Duration: ~2 min to launch, then students build ~28-38 min]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Display the three tier cards. Read the goals clearly, then release them. This
is the heart of the session -- the longest block. Circulate constantly, coaching
not correcting.]

"Now it's YOUR turn. Same idea -- loops -- your own project. Find your tier goal
on the slide. Everyone succeeds at their own level, and fast finishers grab a
challenge card instead of waiting."

"TIER A -- Grades 1-3: Use 'repeat 4' to draw a SQUARE, just like we did
together. That's the whole goal -- a loop that makes a shape. Buddy pairs
welcome and encouraged.
   GOAL: a 'repeat 4' loop that draws a square (a preview of Python turtle!)."

"TIER B -- Grades 3-5: Pick YOUR sprite. Make BOTH kinds of loop -- a 'repeat'
loop that draws a shape or a repeating pattern, AND a 'forever' loop that
animates or moves your sprite. Name your project!
   GOAL: one 'repeat N' pattern PLUS one 'forever' animation, on your own sprite."

"TIER C -- Grades 6-9: NESTED LOOPS. Put one loop INSIDE another to draw a grid
of shapes or a spiral. An outer loop that repeats the whole shape, an inner loop
that draws each shape.
   CHALLENGE CARD: change the outer 'repeat' to 12 and the turn to 30 degrees --
   what shape do you get? (Hint: a mandala / snowflake.)"

"FINISHED EARLY? Count how many lines your loop SAVED and write it on a sticky
note -- we'll find whose loop saved the most. Or add a sound on each loop, help
a neighbor debug, or reach for the next tier. Nobody sits and waits in this room."

[KEY IDEA -- say before releasing them]:
"One coaching rule for open build, same as yesterday: PREDICT before you click.
Before you press the green flag, say out loud how many times you THINK your loop
will run, and what shape you'll get. Then click. If it surprises you -- if you
get a triangle instead of a square -- you just found the most valuable thing in
the room: a gap in your understanding. That surprise is where the learning is."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 14 -- OPEN BUILD COACHING NOTES + CHALLENGE CARD (NESTED LOOPS)
[Use while circulating -- coaching notes not projected; challenge card shown]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[Move constantly. Kneel to eye level. Praise effort and process out loud; give
any correction quietly and privately -- this cohort is sensitive to public
criticism. Display the "Challenge Card - Nested Loops" mandala image for Tier C.]

CHALLENGE CARD SCRIPT (Tier C, say quietly to fast finishers):
"A nested loop is a loop inside a loop. Outer loop: 'repeat 12.' Inside it, put
your whole square-drawing loop, then a 'turn 30 degrees.' Now you draw a square,
rotate 30 degrees, and repeat that twelve times -- twelve squares fanned around
a center. That mandala on the slide? Nested loops made it. Twelve times thirty
is 360 -- a full circle of squares. Try changing 12 and 30 and watch the pattern
transform."

COMMON ISSUES & QUICK FIXES:
  - Shape doesn't close / looks wrong -> "Check your turn angle. Sides times
    turn must equal 360. A square needs turn 90, not 100." (Point at the number,
    let them fix it and feel the win.)
  - Cat draws offscreen / nothing appears -> "Add 'go to x:0 y:0' at the top,
    and make sure 'pen down' is in the script. No pen down, no line."
  - Blocks sit OUTSIDE the loop's mouth -> "They need to be INSIDE the C-shape.
    Drag them into the mouth until the loop's arms hug them." (The #1 loop bug.)
  - 'forever' loop won't stop -> "That's correct! Forever never stops on its own
    -- click the red stop sign." (Turn the 'bug' into the lesson.)
  - Old drawing stays on screen -> "Add 'erase all' (Pen block) at the very top
    so each run starts clean."
  - "I'm done!" (too fast) -> hand them the nested-loop challenge card or the
    line-saved count; never let them idle.

WATCH FOR (celebrate publicly):
  - A student who changes ONE number and gets a new shape -- name it: "You just
    changed a loop and changed everything -- that's the power of loops!"
  - A student debugging their own turn-angle -- "You just DEBUGGED! That's what
    real coders do all day."
  - Kind buddy behavior -- praise it loudly; it sets the culture.

TIER A support: keep them on the square; success = a closed square from
'repeat 4.' Buddy them and celebrate loudly when it closes.
TIER C stretch: nested loops -> ask "what would a loop inside a loop inside a
loop draw?" (seeds deeper structure -- and connects to Python turtle later).


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 14b -- 2-MINUTE WARNING & SAVE
[Duration: woven into the end of open build]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

"Two minutes, coders! Bring your project to a good stopping point. And IMPORTANT
-- SAVE your work: File menu, a screenshot, or copy your project link. We never
lose the thing we just built."

================================================================================
APPENDIX A -- COMMON PITFALLS & HOW TO HANDLE THEM
================================================================================

PITFALL: Blocks are there but nothing repeats.
  Handle: "Are your action blocks INSIDE the loop's mouth -- hugged by the
          C-shape arms? A block sitting below the loop, not inside it, runs only
          once. Drag it up into the mouth until the arms wrap around it."

PITFALL: The square won't close -- it looks lopsided or spirals.
  Handle: "Check the turn angle. Sides times turn must equal 360. Four sides
          need turn 90. If you typed 100, four turns overshoot and it won't
          close. Change it to 90 and watch it snap shut."

PITFALL: "I clicked the flag but I see no drawing."
  Handle: "Two things. Is 'pen down' at the top of the script? No pen, no line.
          And is the cat drawing offscreen? Add 'go to x:0 y:0' at the top so it
          starts in the middle every time."

PITFALL: The 'forever' loop won't stop and the student thinks it's broken.
  Handle: "That's not a bug -- that's the definition! 'forever' never stops on
          its own. Click the red stop sign next to the green flag. That's the
          whole difference between 'forever' and 'repeat.'"

PITFALL: Each run stacks a new messy drawing on the old one.
  Handle: "Add an 'erase all' block (Pen category) at the very top of your
          green-flag script, so every run starts on a clean stage."

PITFALL: Young student (Grade 1-2) overwhelmed by choosing a number.
  Handle: Narrow the world. "Today we only need one number: 4, for four sides.
          Move, turn, inside 'repeat 4.' That's the whole square." Buddy them
          and celebrate the moment it closes.

PITFALL: Fast Tier-C student finishes the square in 3 minutes and is bored.
  Handle: Hand the nested-loop challenge card immediately: "A loop inside a loop
          -- draw a square, turn 30, repeat 12. You'll get a mandala." (Seeds
          the structure they'll meet again in Python turtle.) Or invite them to
          mentor a younger buddy.

PITFALL: "Why use a loop? I could just copy the blocks."
  Handle: "Let's test it. Copy 'move, turn' four times by hand -- now change the
          shape to a hexagon. You have to edit eight blocks. With a loop you
          change TWO numbers. Same result, way less work, way fewer mistakes.
          That's why loops exist."


================================================================================
APPENDIX B -- STRETCH QUESTIONS FOR TIER C / ADVANCED STUDENTS
================================================================================

1. THE 360 RULE: Any regular shape you draw with a loop follows sides x turn =
   360. A triangle turns 120, a pentagon turns 72. What happens as the turn
   angle gets tiny -- say 'repeat 360, turn 1'? (Answer: it becomes a circle.
   A circle is just a polygon with a huge number of sides.)

2. NESTED LOOPS: A loop inside a loop. If the outer loop runs 12 times and the
   inner loop runs 4 times, how many total inner steps run? (Answer: 12 x 4 = 48.
   Seeds multiplication-as-nesting and Big-O intuition later.)

3. forever = repeat until: 'forever' is really "repeat until the user quits."
   Next session we learn conditions. How would you write a loop that stops when
   the score hits 10? (Seeds 'repeat until' and conditionals -- Session 5.)

4. AI CONNECTION: We said AI loops through examples to learn. If an AI loops
   1,000 times vs 1,000,000 times, which learns the pattern better -- and is
   there a point where MORE looping stops helping? (Real ML idea: diminishing
   returns / overfitting. Great intuition for Session 15.)

5. EFFICIENCY: A loop drawing 100 squares runs the same 2 blocks 100 times.
   If each block took 1 second, how long is the loop? Now imagine an AI looping
   over a billion photos. Why do real AIs need very fast computers? (Seeds the
   idea of compute scale.)


================================================================================
APPENDIX C -- QUICK REFERENCE: TODAY'S KEY IDEAS
================================================================================

LOOP:      An instruction that repeats a group of steps multiple times.
           Fewer lines, fewer bugs, easy to change. (Jumping jacks / buttering.)

repeat N:  Runs a FIXED number of times, then stops on its own.
           Use it when you can COUNT the repeats. (repeat 4 = a square.)

forever:   Runs and NEVER stops on its own -- only the red stop sign ends it.
           Use it when the action should never end. (A game's motion, a dance.)

THE LOOP BLOCKS LIVE IN:  the ORANGE Control category.

INSIDE THE MOUTH:  Only blocks placed INSIDE a loop's C-shaped mouth repeat.
           Blocks outside the mouth run once.

THE 360 RULE:  For a shape, (number of sides) x (turn angle) = 360.
           Square: 4 x 90. Triangle: 3 x 120. Hexagon: 6 x 60.

NESTED LOOP:  A loop inside a loop -- repeats a whole pattern (grids, mandalas).

THE ONE-LINE TAKEAWAY:
           "Why write it 10 times when once will do? -- Use a loop."

AI TIE-IN: "AI learns by repeating." An AI LOOPS through thousands of examples,
           getting a little better each time around. Your 'repeat' block IS the
           training loop of an AI: more loops = more practice = a smarter model.
