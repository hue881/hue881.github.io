SLIDE 3 — CORE CONCEPT: TOKENIZATION
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the tokenization slide with the colored token cards and the
Python tokenizer code. Point to the token visualization as you speak.]

"Every AI that processes language — from TinyBot to GPT-4 — starts
with the same first step: tokenization. Before any analysis can
happen, the AI must break the input into manageable pieces called
tokens."

"A token is the smallest unit of meaning that the system works with.
In our version today, tokens are whole words. We lowercase everything
and strip punctuation, then split on spaces. So the sentence
'I am feeling really excited today!' becomes the list:
['i', 'am', 'feeling', 'really', 'excited', 'today']"

"Look at the colored token cards on the slide. Each word becomes its
own labeled box — its own unit the program can examine. The
exclamation mark is gone. The capital I is now lowercase i. This
process of cleaning and splitting is our tokenizer."

💡 [DEEP EXPLANATION]:
"Now here is where it gets genuinely interesting. Our tokenizer uses
whole words. GPT-4 uses something called Byte-Pair Encoding — BPE
for short — which breaks words into sub-word pieces. Look at the
slide's second example. The word 'feeling' becomes two tokens: 'feel'
and 'ing.' The word 'excited' becomes three: 'ex,' 'cit,' and 'ed.'

Why would anyone do this? Because of the vocabulary explosion problem.
English has hundreds of thousands of words. If you treat each whole
word as a separate token, your vocabulary table becomes enormous and
full of rare words the model has barely seen. But most of those
words are built from common roots, prefixes, and suffixes. BPE
discovers the most frequently occurring sub-word pieces in the
training data and uses those as the vocabulary — typically around
50,000 pieces. This way, any word in any language can be
reconstructed from familiar parts, and nothing is truly 'unknown.'

GPT-4 was trained on 13 trillion tokens. Each token is roughly a
word or word-piece. That is approximately 10 trillion words —
equivalent to every article on Wikipedia read 50,000 times over."

[Point to the Python tokenizer code on the slide:]

"Here is our tokenizer — three lines. We use a regular expression
to strip anything that is not a letter or space, lowercase everything,
and split on spaces. Clean, simple, and good enough for what we
need today."

✋ [Ask students]: "Look at the code. The regex removes apostrophes.
   So 'I'm' becomes 'im' and 'not' stays 'not.' Are those semantically
   the same? What gets lost when we remove the apostrophe? How would
   you fix it?"
   [Expected insight: 'im' and 'i am' are now different tokens even
   though they mean the same thing. Contractions lose their meaning
   markers. A smarter tokenizer would expand contractions first.]

SLIDE 4 — CORE CONCEPT: BAG-OF-WORDS SCORING
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the bag-of-words scoring slide. The INTENTS dictionary and
the classify function should be visible. Walk through both slowly.]

"Now we move from tokenization to classification. How does TinyBot
decide what a message means? Version 1 used if-elif chains. Version
2 uses a smarter approach: scoring."

"Instead of asking 'is the word sad in this message — yes or no?'
we assign numerical weights to keywords and compute a score for
each intent. The intent with the highest score wins."

"Look at the INTENTS dictionary on the slide. The 'negative' intent
has weights: 'sad' scores 3, 'tired' scores 2, 'upset' scores 3,
'unhappy' scores 3, 'depressed' scores 4. The classify function
loops over every intent, checks every token in the message, and
adds up the matching weights. Highest total wins."

[Walk through the trace example on the slide — 'I feel a bit sad
and tired':]

"Let's trace this together. Tokens: ['i', 'feel', 'a', 'bit', 'sad',
'and', 'tired']. Greeting score: 0 — no matching keywords. Negative
score: sad adds 3, tired adds 2 — total 5. Positive score: 0.
Winner: negative with score 5. Correct."

💡 [DEEP INSIGHT]:
"This approach — representing a piece of text as a set of weighted
word counts — is called the Bag-of-Words model, or BoW. It is one
of the oldest and most studied ideas in NLP, dating to the 1950s.
The name is deliberately humble: you take a sentence, throw all the
words into a bag, shake it up, and look at what you have — with no
regard for order, structure, or grammar. Just which words are present,
and how many times.

BoW works surprisingly well for simple classification tasks — spam
detection, basic sentiment analysis, topic categorization. But it
has a fundamental limitation baked into its design: it has no memory
of word order. The sentences 'The dog bit the man' and 'The man bit
the dog' produce identical bags. This is why, for anything requiring
real understanding, modern systems moved to word embeddings and then
to Transformer architectures that explicitly model the relationship
between every word and every other word in the sentence."

[Point to the broken example on the slide:]

"But here is the crack in the foundation. 'I'm not sad at all, I
feel great!' Scoring: sad adds 3 to negative, great adds 2 to
positive. Negative wins — score 3 to 2. Wrong answer. The word 'not'
should have flipped the entire meaning. But our bag does not track
word order, so 'not' and 'sad' are just two separate keywords
floating in the same bag."

[Point to the negation window code on the slide:]

"The fix shown here is called a negation window. For each token,
we look one position to the left. If the previous token is 'not,'
'n't,' or 'never,' we flip the score from positive to negative.
It is a patch — not a real solution — but it handles the most
common cases and teaches you the idea."

✋ [Ask students]: "Can you think of a sentence where the negation
   window still fails?" [Expected: 'not even close to being sad'
   — the negation word is too far from the emotion word. Or 'I would
   never say I'm not happy' — double negation that even humans argue
   about.]

SLIDE 5 — THE CODE: TINYBOT FULLY ANNOTATED
[Duration: ~7 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the full TinyBot v1 code block. Read through it completely
line by line BEFORE students touch a keyboard. Do not rush this —
every line has something worth explaining, and several lines contain
intentional bugs that are pedagogically valuable.]

"Here is TinyBot v1. The original. Read it with me — do not type
yet. Every line."

"Line 1: print a welcome message. Straightforward.

Line 2: while True — an infinite loop. This is the event loop.
The program runs forever, waiting for input, responding, then
waiting again. Every interactive program you have ever used — every
app, every game, every website — has an event loop at its core. This
is yours.

Line 3: msg = input('You: ').lower().strip() — three operations
chained together. input() pauses execution and waits for the user
to type. .lower() converts everything to lowercase so 'Hello' and
'hello' are treated identically. .strip() removes leading and
trailing whitespace so accidental spaces do not break matching."

"Now the conditional chain. The first check is for 'bye' — an exact
match. If the user types exactly 'bye,' we print goodbye and break
out of the loop. break is the only way to exit a while True loop
gracefully from the inside."

"Then: elif 'hello' in msg or 'hi' in msg — and here we hit our
first bug, helpfully marked in a comment on the slide."

[Point to the BUG annotation:]

"The substring 'hi' appears inside the words 'this,' 'white,' and
'hi-five.' If a user types 'I saw this movie,' TinyBot responds
'Hi there!' because the substring 'hi' is hiding inside the word
'this.' The computer does not know the difference between a standalone
word and a piece of a larger word — it just finds the character
sequence. This is the substring trap."

"The fix is tokenization: instead of 'hi' in msg, check 'hi' in
msg.split() — which only returns True if 'hi' is a complete token.
One character change. Enormous behavioral difference."

[Continue through the elif chain:]

"Next bug: elif 'sad' in msg or 'tired' in msg. As we just saw, this
fires on 'I'm not sad at all.' No negation awareness whatsoever.
First match wins, no scoring, no nuance."

💡 [KEY ARCHITECTURAL INSIGHT]:
"Notice the structure of this code. It is a chain of elif statements —
Python evaluates them top to bottom and stops at the first match.
This means order is policy. If a message contains both 'hi' and 'sad,'
whichever branch comes first in the code wins, regardless of which
intent is stronger. This is called first-match bias, and it is a
silent decision built into the code structure that most beginners
never notice.

Professional systems replace the elif chain with a scoring function
that evaluates all intents in parallel and selects the highest score.
That is exactly what v2 on the next slide does. The improvement is
not just cosmetic — it changes the fundamental decision architecture
from sequential to comparative."

[Walk through the four annotated problem cards on the slide:]

"Substring bug — fix with .split(). First-match bias — fix with
scoring. Statelessness — the bot has zero memory between turns, every
loop iteration starts fresh with no knowledge of what was said before.
And the challenge on the slide: how many distinct inputs produce
an incorrect reply? Is that number finite or infinite? Think about it."

✋ [Ask students to answer the challenge: finite or infinite? Expected
   answer: infinite — because there are infinitely many messages
   containing 'not sad' that will be incorrectly classified as negative.
   Any prefix before a keyword creates a new broken case.]

💡 [SYNTHESIS — say this after student responses]:
"Here is the key insight from everything you just watched. The video
bot and TinyBot v1 share the same fundamental architecture: a
conditional chain that checks keywords one by one. This approach is
called a rule-based system or deterministic system. Given the same
input, it always produces the same output — no randomness, no
learning, no adaptation.

The word 'deterministic' is worth pausing on. It means the system
is perfectly predictable and perfectly debuggable. You can trace
every decision. You can write tests that cover every branch. This
is a strength — in safety-critical systems, predictability is more
valuable than flexibility.

But determinism is also the ceiling. A deterministic system can
only respond to what its programmer anticipated. Every novel phrasing,
every regional dialect, every new slang word is a gap. The moment a
user says something the programmer did not predict, the system falls
into the else branch: 'I don't understand.' Modern systems are
probabilistic — they assign confidence scores to every possible
intent and pick the most likely one, even for inputs never seen
before. That is a completely different design philosophy."

SLIDE 7 — CODE: INTENT CLASSIFIER v2 WITH DICTIONARIES
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the v2 dictionary-driven classifier code. The RULES dict,
REPLIES dict, and getintent function should all be visible. Walk
through each section deliberately.]

"TinyBot v2 does the same job as v1, but the design is fundamentally
better. Let's see why."

"First, RULES. Instead of hardcoded elif conditions, we have a
dictionary that maps each intent name to a list of keywords. Adding
a new intent requires zero changes to the program logic — you just
add one line to the dictionary. This is called data-driven design."

"Second, REPLIES. Another dictionary — maps intent names to response
strings. A content editor could update all the replies without
reading a single line of logic code. The language of the bot and the
behavior of the bot are completely separated."

"Third, getintent. This is our classifier function. It tokenizes the
message into a set — not a list, a set — and then loops over every
intent in RULES. The line 'if tokens & set(kws)' uses Python's set
intersection operator. If even one token from the message matches
one keyword from the intent's list, this intent fires."

💡 [DATA STRUCTURES DEPTH]:
"Why a set instead of a list? Membership testing. Checking whether
a value exists in a list takes O(n) time — Python must scan every
element from the start. Checking membership in a set takes O(1)
average time — Python uses a hash table that jumps directly to the
relevant bucket. For a list of 1,000 keywords, sets are roughly
50 times faster per lookup.

The set intersection operator — the ampersand — returns a new set
containing only elements present in both sets. If that set is
non-empty, there is at least one keyword match. This single line
replaces an entire for-loop with a single, readable, mathematically
precise operation.

This is also why Python has both lists and sets — they are optimized
for different operations. Lists are ordered and support indexing.
Sets are unordered and optimized for membership and intersection.
Choosing the right data structure for the task at hand is one of
the most important decisions in software engineering."

[Walk through the batch classification example on the slide:]

"Here we classify three messages in a loop — 'hey there,' 'I am so
unhappy,' 'goodbye!' — and print the intent and reply for each.
Three lines of application code, sitting on top of a clean,
extensible, data-driven engine. This is what well-structured code
looks like."

[Point to the 'still first-match biased' card:]

"But notice: the for loop in getintent stops at the first matching
intent. We have replaced the elif chain with a for loop — but the
first-match bias is still there. It is just harder to see.
Challenge: how would you modify getintent to return ALL matching
intents, ranked by score? Sketch the algorithm — we will need it
for the exercise."

SLIDE 8 — CS THEORY: FINITE STATE MACHINES
[Duration: ~5 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the FSM slide with the four-state diagram and the FSM code.
Trace the diagram on the board with your finger as you explain each
state and transition.]

"TinyBot has a fundamental limitation we have not solved yet:
statelessness. Every time the while loop runs, the bot starts
completely fresh. It has no memory of your name, no memory of what
you said two turns ago, no awareness that it already greeted you.
It is like talking to someone with complete amnesia after every
sentence."

"The minimal fix for this is a Finite State Machine — FSM. Let's
look at one."

[Point to the four-node FSM diagram:]

"Four states: START, ASK_NAME, KNOW_NAME, and END. We add one
variable called 'state' that persists between loop iterations.
When state is 'start' and the user greets, we respond and
transition to 'askname.' When state is 'askname,' we capture
whatever the user types as their name and transition to
'knowname.' When state is 'knowname,' we use the stored name in
every reply. When the user says bye, we transition to END and break."

"The critical word is transition. The FSM is a map of allowed
paths through a conversation. Not every input is valid at every
state — 'what is your name' is only meaningful in the 'askname'
state. In all other states, it is ignored or redirected."

💡 [THEORETICAL DEPTH]:
"An FSM has a formal mathematical definition. It is a 5-tuple:
Q, Σ, δ, q₀, F. Q is the set of all possible states. Σ is the
input alphabet — in our case, the set of all possible tokens.
δ is the transition function: given the current state and an
input, δ tells you the next state. q₀ is the starting state.
F is the set of accepting states — states that represent a
successfully completed conversation.

TinyBot v1 has exactly one state: always START, after every
turn. Our FSM chatbot has four states. A modern voice assistant
like Siri may have thousands of states and sub-states — for
booking a restaurant, playing a song, setting a timer, answering
a question — each with their own valid transitions.

FSMs are one of the oldest models in theoretical computer science,
dating to the 1950s. They are the formal underpinning of regular
expressions, network protocols, compiler design, and game AI.
Every traffic light you have ever waited at is a finite state
machine. Every vending machine. Every elevator. They are everywhere
— and now you can build one."

[Walk through the FSM code line by line:]

roughly 175 billion parameters performing billions of multiply-add
operations per token. The gulf between them is not a matter of
degree — it is a matter of architectural philosophy."

"TinyBot classifies intent using keyword presence. A Transformer

updates its meaning accordingly. The same mechanism handles
pronouns, coreference, negation, and irony — things no keyword
system can touch."

💡 [THE ARCHITECTURAL LEAP]:
"Here is the connection that should tie today's lesson together.
TinyBot's 'hi' in msg.split() is a lookup: does this token exist
in this set? Zero context. One bit of information.

BERT's embedding of the word 'hi' is a 768-dimensional vector that
encodes not just what the word is, but what it means in this
particular sentence, in this particular context, having seen all
the other words simultaneously.

The difference between one bit and 768 dimensions of context is
the difference between TinyBot and a language model that passes the
bar exam. The concepts are the same — tokenize, classify, respond.
The mathematics underneath is incomparably richer.

Your code today is not a toy. It is the conceptual foundation. And
understanding the foundation is the most important first step in
understanding everything built on top of it."

"SARCASM: 'Oh great, another Monday.' TinyBot sees the word 'great'

"COREFERENCE: 'My dog is sick. She needs a vet.' TinyBot has no

tracking which entities have been mentioned and which attributes
they carry across turns. This is a hard NLP problem called
coreference resolution."

"WORLD KNOWLEDGE: 'I am going to the Apple store.' TinyBot cannot
distinguish Apple the company from apple the fruit without external
world knowledge. Every entity in every sentence exists in a
context that extends far beyond the conversation itself."

💡 "These are not bugs you can fix with more elif statements. They
are fundamental properties of language — ambiguity, context-
dependence, world-knowledge-dependence — that require richer models
of meaning than any pattern matcher can provide. This is why NLP
is still an active research field. This is why ChatGPT cost hundreds
of millions of dollars to train. Language is the hardest thing
humans do. Building machines that do it well is correspondingly hard."

PITFALL: Student asks "why can't we just put all the elif rules in a
         bigger dictionary?"
  Handle: "That is exactly what v2 does — and it is a genuine
          improvement. But notice that even v2's for loop has first-
          match bias. Making the dictionary bigger does not solve the
          scoring problem. The fix requires not just a different data
          structure but a different algorithm: evaluate all intents,
          accumulate scores, return the highest. Bigger rules plus
          the same algorithm is just a slower version of the same
          mistake."

PITFALL: Student cannot see why set intersection is faster than a
         for loop.
  Handle: "Let me give you the concrete version. You have 1,000
          keywords and a message with 10 words. A list intersection
          does 10,000 comparisons — 10 tokens against 1,000 keywords.
          A set intersection computes the hash of each of your 10 tokens
          — 10 hash lookups — and checks whether each hash exists in
          the keyword hash table. 10 operations versus 10,000.
          At scale, this is the difference between a bot that responds
          in milliseconds and one that takes seconds."

PITFALL: Student confused about why while True doesn't crash
         immediately.
  Handle: "While True is an intentionally infinite loop — it only
          exits via an explicit break statement. This is the standard
          pattern for any event-driven program: wait for input,
          process it, respond, wait again. The break on the 'bye'
          branch is the only designed exit. Every interactive
          program you use — your phone's home screen, your browser,
          your operating system — is running an event loop right
          now. It is while True, handled with extraordinary
          sophistication."

PITFALL: Student asks "isn't ChatGPT just a really big TinyBot?"
  Handle: "That question is worth taking seriously. The answer is:
          no, qualitatively, not just quantitatively. TinyBot looks
          up pre-written responses by matching keywords. ChatGPT
          generates new text token by token, using a probability
          distribution over the entire vocabulary at each step,
          conditioned on all prior tokens. It never retrieves a
          pre-written string — it synthesizes a new one. The process
          is more like a musician improvising than a jukebox
          selecting a song. That is not a difference of scale.
          That is a difference of architecture."

PITFALL: Student's FSM code breaks when state transitions happen
         in the wrong order.
  Handle: "Draw the diagram again. Look at every outgoing arrow.
          Now look at your code. Does every transition in the code
          match exactly one arrow in the diagram? Missing arrow
          in the diagram means missing elif in the code — and
          vice versa. The diagram is the specification. The code
          implements it. If they disagree, the bug is wherever
          they disagree."

PITFALL: Student asks "why do we lowercase everything if
         capitalization can matter — like proper names?"
  Handle: "Excellent catch — this is a genuine tradeoff. Lowercasing
          gives us case-insensitive matching, which is almost always
          what we want for intent keywords. But it also means we lose
          the signal that a capitalized word might be a proper noun.
          'Apple' the company and 'apple' the fruit get the same token.
          Professional tokenizers handle this with named-entity
          recognition — a separate process that identifies proper
          nouns before lowercasing. For TinyBot, the tradeoff is
          worth it — the simplicity gain outweighs the precision loss.
          At production scale, you handle both."

APPENDIX C — QUICK REFERENCE: KEY FORMULAS & DEFINITIONS
================================================================================

Bag-of-Words Score:
  score(intent) = Σ weight(token) for each token in message
                  where token ∈ INTENTS[intent]

Negation Window:
  sign = −1 if tokens[i−1] ∈ {not, n't, never} else +1
  score(intent) += sign × weight(token)

Set Intersection (Python):
  tokens & set(kws)  →  O(min(|tokens|, |kws|)) average
  vs. list scan      →  O(|tokens| × |kws|)

FSM Formal Definition:
  M = (Q, Σ, δ, q₀, F)
  Q = {start, askname, knowname, end}
  Σ = set of all possible input tokens
  δ : Q × Σ → Q  (transition function)
  q₀ = start
  F = {end}

Big-O of elif chain:
  O(R) where R = number of rules, assuming constant-time comparisons
  O(R × W) if each rule scans a keyword list of length W
  O(1) amortized for dictionary lookup + O(min(n,m)) for set intersection
